## Overview
Head position in backstroke is critical because it directly controls body and hip position. The head should rest with the water line at ear level, looking straight up at the ceiling or sky. A stable, neutral head position keeps the hips high and maintains a streamlined body line.

**Difficulty:** Easiest

## Key Points to Remember
- **Water line at ears** - Head should rest with water at ear level
- **Look straight up** - Eyes focused on ceiling/sky, not forward
- **Chin slightly tucked** - Neutral neck position
- **Head still** - No bobbing or weaving
- **Trust your stroke** - Don't lift head to look around

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Looking forward** - Raises head, drops hips immediately
- **Head too deep** - Water washes over face, uncomfortable
- **Head bobbing** - Disrupts stroke rhythm
- **Craning neck to see** - Breaks streamline
- **Tense neck** - Causes fatigue and poor position

## Specific Drills

| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Cup Drill** | Place water bottle on forehead, keep it balanced while swimming |
| **Head Lead** | Swim with head lead, arms at sides, focus on head stillness |
| **Lane Line Guide** | Use lane lines overhead to stay oriented without looking |
| **Mirror Drill** | Visualize ceiling as mirror, maintain eye contact |
| **Still Head Focus** | 25m sets focusing purely on head stability |

## Competitive Drills

#### Drill 1: Cup Balance Drill (200m continuous swim)
**Self-Check:** Count percentage of laps completed without spilling water from a plastic cup balanced on your forehead

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥50% of laps without spilling
> - **Intermediate:** ≥75% of laps
> - **Advanced:** ≥90% of laps
> - **Elite:** ≥95% of laps (100% is an ideal target but may be unrealistic in practice)
>
> Self-check cue: Keep your eyes fixed on a point directly above you, no looking side to side
>
> **Note:** These percentages are coaching heuristics. Accurately counting spill percentages during swimming is challenging without video analysis.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Head angle: ≤10 degrees tilt from horizontal (visual estimate — head should appear level)
- No head movement: Head stays stable throughout entire stroke cycle (minor movement is normal and unavoidable)
- Competitive target: Ears remain at water surface for majority of strokes

**Competitive Impact:** Head lift drops hips, increasing drag. The commonly cited "1cm head lift drops hips 5cm" ratio is a **coaching heuristic** (not a fixed mechanical law) — the actual relationship varies by body proportions, torso length, and individual buoyancy distribution. Research by Yanai and Caldwell (1999) confirms the head-hip connection through the body's center of mass and center of buoyancy. The resulting drag increase can cost approximately 0.5-1.0s per 100m for moderate head position errors. *Exact values vary by body type and baseline technique.*

#### Drill 2: Blind Stroke Count Drill (50m swim, eyes closed)
**Self-Check:** Count your strokes per 50m with eyes closed, compare to normal open-eye count

> [!note] Targets
> - Competitive target: ≤2 stroke difference between closed and open eyes. **Note:** ≤1 stroke difference is an elite-level target that is difficult to verify with hand timing.
> - Self-check cue: Keep your head completely still, use body awareness to feel strokes
> - **Safety note:** Only attempt in a clear lane with no nearby swimmers. Eyes-closed swimming requires a safe environment.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- No head lift/tuck: Head position similar to open-eye swimming
- No neck strain: Neutral neck position, no forward jutting
- Competitive target: No major change in stroke rhythm when eyes are closed

**Competitive Impact:** Consistent head position eliminates wasted energy on constant balance adjustments. This drill primarily develops **proprioception** (body awareness) rather than directly improving race times.
