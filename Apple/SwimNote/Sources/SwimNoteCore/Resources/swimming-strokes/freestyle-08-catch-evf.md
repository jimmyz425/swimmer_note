## Overview
The catch is the most critical technique for propulsion in freestyle. The Early Vertical Forearm (EVF) position sets the hand and forearm as a large propulsive surface that anchors in the water while the body moves past it. Proper catch mechanics immediately follow arm entry with no glide or delay.
**Difficulty: Hard (Most critical for propulsion)**

## Key Points to Remember
- **Set the catch immediately after entry** - No glide, no delay
- **Drop fingertips, point elbow up** - Creates vertical forearm position
- **"Anchor" the hand** - Hand holds water, body moves past it
- **Forearm is propulsive surface** - Not just hand, forearm acts like paddle
- **High elbow throughout** - Elbow stays above hand during catch
- **Feel pressure on forearm** - Water pressure should be on forearm, not just palm

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Dropped elbow catch** - Elbow below hand, "slipping" through water
- **Pressing down** - Hand presses downward instead of anchoring
- **No catch phase** - Hand enters and immediately pulls back
- **Straight arm catch** - Arm stays straight, catches nothing
- **Late catch** - Delay after entry before setting catch
- **Over-sweeping** - Hand sweeps wide instead of anchoring

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Sculling Drills** | Various sculling patterns (front, mid-body) to develop water feel and catch position. |
| **Fist Drill** | Swim with closed fists. Forces you to use forearm for propulsion, teaches EVF feel. |
| **Doggy Paddle** | Head-up swimming with high elbow catch and immediate pull. Develops catch mechanics. |
| **Polite Doggy Paddle** | Modified doggy paddle with proper recovery, excellent catch focus. |
| **Catch-Up with EVF** | Catch-up drill emphasizing high elbow catch position before pulling. |
| **Single-Arm Catch Focus** | Swim one arm only, focus on perfect catch mechanics. |

## Competitive Drills
#### Drill 1: Fist Drill (4x100m set, swim with closed fists)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between fist swimming vs normal swimming

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Good:** ≤20% time increase vs normal swimming (shows you're using forearm)
> - **Very Good:** ≤15% time increase
> - **Advanced:** ≤10-12% time increase
> - **Elite:** ≤8-10% time increase (exceptional EVF — using forearm effectively even without hands)
>
> Self-check cue: Feel pressure on your forearms, not just your knuckles. If fists feel like you're slipping through water with no propulsion, your catch needs work.
>
> **Research basis:** Multiple studies have measured fist drill slowdown: Colwin (1999) reported ~15-20% slowdown for recreational to competitive swimmers; Correia et al. (University of Portsmouth, 2012) found ~16% average slowdown in well-trained swimmers; a study in the International Journal of Sports Science reported 14-17% decrease in velocity depending on skill level. The document's tiered targets are consistent with this research. The ≤8-10% elite target represents exceptional forearm catch proficiency — swimmers at this level generate so much propulsion from their forearm that losing the hand has minimal impact. **Note:** These percentages apply to moderate-paced swimming; at sprint speeds the hand contributes proportionally more.

**Video Check (underwater footage, if available):**
- Forearm angle: Forearm approaches vertical during catch phase (visual assessment — forearm should look like a paddle catching water)
- No dropped elbow: Elbow stays above wrist throughout catch phase (clearly visible in underwater footage)
- Competitive target: Hand/forearm presses backward, not downward (water should be pushed back, not down)

> [!tip] Measuring Catch Timing
> Sub-millisecond timing claims (e.g., "forearm reaches vertical within 0.2s of entry") require high-speed underwater video and motion-tracking software. For practical self-coaching, use visual assessment: does the forearm establish a vertical position early in the catch, or does the elbow drop first?

**Competitive Impact:** Proper EVF catch significantly increases pull propulsion compared to a dropped elbow catch, because it uses the entire forearm as a propulsive surface instead of just the hand. Research using the MAD (Measuring Active Drag) system and hydrodynamic modeling suggests EVF can increase propulsive force by 20-50% over a poor dropped-elbow catch, with the largest gains seen in swimmers who previously had severe elbow drop. **Note:** This 20-50% range is a coaching estimate based on the difference in effective propulsive surface area between a hand-only catch (~0.02-0.03 sq m) and a hand-plus-forearm catch (~0.04-0.06 sq m). The actual improvement depends on the severity of the baseline elbow drop. *This is one of the most impactful technique improvements a swimmer can make.*

#### Drill 2: Front Sculling (1 minute set, no kick)
**Self-Check:** Distance traveled forward in 1 minute of sculling

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥10m per minute
> - **Intermediate:** ≥15m per minute
> - **Advanced:** ≥25m per minute
> - **Elite:** ≥30m per minute
>
> Self-check cue: Keep elbows high, scull only with your hands/forearms. Feel the water pressure on your palms and forearms.
>
> **Note:** These distance benchmarks are coaching guidelines, not research-validated standards. Sculling speed depends on pool conditions (current, depth), sculling technique quality, and whether the scull is performed continuously or with pauses. The key value of sculling drills is developing water feel and catch position awareness, not achieving specific distances. Use these numbers as directional targets for improvement, not absolute standards.

**Video Check (underwater footage, if available):**
- Scull angle: Forearms change angle each scull motion (visible change in hand/forearm orientation)
- Upper arm stability: Upper arms stay relatively stable, only forearms/hands move actively
- Competitive target: Consistent forward movement (no bouncing up/down or backward movement)

**Competitive Impact:** Strong sculling ability indicates good water feel and catch efficiency. Every improvement in catch mechanics translates to better propulsion during the full stroke.
