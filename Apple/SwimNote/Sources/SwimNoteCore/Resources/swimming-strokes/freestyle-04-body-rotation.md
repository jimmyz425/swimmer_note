## Overview
Body rotation is a fundamental freestyle technique where the body rotates around the spine axis (long axis rotation). Proper rotation extends reach, reduces drag, enables breathing, and increases pull power. The body should never stay flat — it should be in continuous, rhythmic rolling motion.
**Difficulty: Moderate-Hard**

## Key Points to Remember
- **Rotate 35-50 degrees on each side** - Not flat, not over-rotated; exact optimal angle varies by body type and event. **Research basis:** Psycharakis & Sanders (2008) measured body roll in elite swimmers and found ranges of approximately 30-60 degrees, with optimal angles depending on event distance, body proportions, and individual technique. Neiva et al. (2013) similarly found rotation angles in the 30-55 degree range for competitive swimmers.
- **Rotate from the hips/core** - Let shoulders follow hip rotation
- **Continuous, rhythmic motion** - Never stay flat; always rolling
- **"Long axis" rotation** - Rotate around the spine axis
- **Rotation extends reach** - Use rotation to extend arm further forward
- **Rotation helps breathing** - Natural body roll brings head to air

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Swimming flat** - No rotation, creates drag and limits reach
- **Over-rotating** - Rolling too far (more than 60 degrees)
- **Rotating only shoulders** - Should move from hips/core first
- **Stopping rotation** - Pause between rotations breaks rhythm
- **Rotating at wrong time** - Timing off relative to arm stroke

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **6-3-6 Drill** | Kick 6 times on right side, take 3 strokes breathing to left, kick 6 times on left side. Master rotation timing. |
| **6-1-6 Drill** | Kick 6 times on side, take 1 stroke, kick 6 times on other side. Focus on smooth rotation transition. *Also covered in [[swimming-strokes/freestyle-02-flutter-kick]] and [[swimming-strokes/freestyle-03-breathing-technique]] sections.* |
| **Single-Arm Rotation** | Swim with one arm only, focusing on how body rotates with each stroke cycle. |
| **Rotation Pause Drill** | Swim normal, pause at 45-degree rotation, feel the position, continue. |
| **Catch-Up with Rotation** | Catch-up drill emphasizing full rotation before each stroke. |

## Competitive Drills
#### Drill 1: 6-1-6 Drill (4x25m set)
**Self-Check:** Stroke count per 25m during the 3-stroke phase of 6-1-6

> [!note] Targets
> - Establish your baseline 6-1-6 stroke count for 25m, then target improvement from that baseline
> - Competitive target: ≤12 strokes during the 3-stroke phase per 25m (rotation extends your reach by 10-15%). **Note:** The 10-15% reach extension estimate is based on the added distance from body rotation allowing the shoulder to extend further forward. This is a coaching estimate, not a precise measurement.
> - Self-check cue: Feel your hip roll before your shoulder starts to move
> - Note: 6-1-6 drill stroke count will be lower than normal swimming due to the kick-pause phases. Do not compare 6-1-6 stroke count directly to full-stroke stroke count.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Rotation angle: 40-50 degrees on each side (visual estimate — body should be clearly rotated but not on its side)
- Hip-led rotation: Hips initiate rotation with shoulders following as a connected unit (look for hip movement preceding or coinciding with shoulder movement)
- Competitive target: 100% of strokes have consistent rotation angle (no stroke-to-stroke variation in body position)

> [!tip] Measuring Rotation
> Sub-millisecond timing (e.g., "hips start 0.05s before shoulders") requires high-speed video (≥200fps). For practical self-coaching, use visual assessment: do hips and shoulders rotate as one unit, or do shoulders twist independently?

**Competitive Impact:** Proper rotation adds pull power and reduces frontal drag. *Published estimates range 5-10% pull power improvement (Psycharakis & Sanders, 2008), but actual gains depend on baseline technique and body type. Body roll reduces frontal cross-sectional area by approximately 10-20% compared to flat swimming, contributing to drag reduction. Rotation also enables better catch position by allowing the shoulder to achieve a higher elbow position. Exact benefits are individual-dependent.*

#### Drill 2: Single-Arm Swimming (4x50m per arm, no kick)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between left and right single-arm swimming

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≤10% time difference between sides
> - **Intermediate:** ≤5% time difference
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≤3% time difference
>
> Self-check cue: Feel your hip rotate to drive the pull, not just your arm

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Unit rotation: Shoulders and hips rotate as a single connected unit (no independent shoulder twisting)
- No cross-over: Hand does not cross center line during pull
- Competitive target: Consistent pull mechanics on both sides (visual comparison of left vs right stroke)

**Competitive Impact:** Side-to-side strength imbalance can cost 2-3% of race speed. **Note:** This figure is a coaching estimate based on observed performance differences between dominant and non-dominant side swimming. Individual variation is significant. Single-arm drills expose asymmetries that are masked during two-arm swimming.
