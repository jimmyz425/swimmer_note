# Backstroke Swimming Technique Reference
## Complete Reference Guide - Ranked from Easy to Hard to Master

> Part of [[areas/swimming-strokes|Swimming Strokes Research]] · Code project: [[projects/swimmer-notes-app|Swimmer's Notes App]]

> [!important] Note on Competitive Targets and Metrics
> All "Competitive target" values in this document are **guidelines**, not absolutes. Actual performance depends on:
> - **Swimmer level** (age-group, collegiate, elite) — tiered targets are provided where applicable
> - **Body type and physiology** — longer limbs, different buoyancy affect metrics
> - **Pool conditions** — water temperature, depth, and current affect times
> - **Measurement method** — hand-timed results have ±0.3s variability; phone video cannot measure sub-centimeter or sub-100ms precision
>
> Metrics requiring high-speed video, motion tracking, or calibrated measurement are noted as such. Focus on **directional improvement** rather than hitting exact numbers.

> [!note] On Difficulty Rankings
> These rankings are general guidelines. Actual difficulty varies by swimmer:
> - **Body Position** (Level 1) and **Head Position** (Level 2) are foundational and closely related — head position directly controls body position in backstroke.
> - **Flutter Kick** (Level 3): Often harder for adult learners than it appears — hip-driven motion is counterintuitive.
> - **Arm Pull/Push** (Level 9) and **Arm Catch** (Level 8): Closely tied together. Many coaches treat these as one combined technique. If your catch is good, the pull often follows naturally.

### Image References

**Key Positions to Visualize:**

1. **Backstroke Body Position**
   - Body flat on surface
   - Head resting, water at ears
   - Hips high, near surface
   - Think: "Floating on a raft" - relaxed, horizontal

2. **Pinky-First Entry**
   - Little finger leads into water
   - Hand rotated outward
   - Arm extended straight back
   - Think: "Slicing pinky into water"

3. **Head Position (Neutral)**
   - Looking straight up at ceiling
   - Water line at ears
   - Chin slightly tucked
   - Think: "Balancing a cup on forehead"

4. **Rotation Angle (30-45°)**
   - One shoulder slightly higher
   - Hips and shoulders roll together
   - Not flat, not over-rotated
   - Think: "Gentle roll, not a flip"
   - Note: Research by Psycharakis & Sanders (2008) measured elite swimmers at ~30-60 degrees. The 30-45 degree target is the commonly coached optimal range; individual optimal angle depends on event, body type, and flexibility.

5. **Bent-Arm Recovery (Corrected)**
   - Elbow leads recovery, hand trails below elbow
   - Arm is bent during recovery, NOT straight
   - Arm stays close to body line
   - Hand rotates from thumb (exit) to pinky (entry)
   - Think: "Elbow lifts first, relaxed hand follows"

6. **High-Elbow Pull Path (Corrected)**
   - Hand pulls relatively straight back (not exaggerated S-curve)
   - Elbow stays above hand during catch and early pull
   - Accelerate through the pull phase
   - Finish at hip level, not past
   - Think: "Anchor and pull body past your hand"

**Research Literature:**
- Maglischo, E.W. (2003) - *Swimming Fastest*. Human Kinetics. (Comprehensive backstroke biomechanics, body position, kick mechanics, pull paths)

- Schleihauf, R.E. (1979) - A hydrodynamic analysis of swimming propulsion. *Swimming III*. (Hand paths, pull path analysis, 3D motion analysis)
- Schleihauf, R.E. et al. (1989) - Three-dimensional analysis of hand propulsion in swimming. (Hand path deviations, force vectors)
- Sanders, R.H. (1999-2007) - Multiple studies on stroke technique and 3D kinematics. (Pull path analysis, body rotation, S-curve vs. straight pull)
- Psycharakis, S.G. & Sanders, R. (2008) - Body roll and its contribution to propulsion in swimming. *Sports Biomechanics*. (Optimal rotation range 30-60 degrees)
- Yanai, T. & Caldwell, G.E. (1999) - Body position and balance in swimming. *Journal of Biomechanics*. (Head-hip relationship, body alignment)
- Bixler, B. & Rieweg, S. (1998) - CFD analysis of underwater swimming drag. *Journal of Biomechanics*. (Drag coefficients, body position effects)
- Bixler, B. et al. (2007) - Computational fluid dynamics in swimming. *Sports Biomechanics*. (CFD drag analysis updates)
- Lyttle, A. et al. (1998) - The importance of the start and turn in competitive swimming. *Sports Engineering*. (Push-off velocity ~1.8-2.5 m/s, underwater phase)
- Lyttle, A. et al. (1999) - Key parameters for the start and turn in competitive swimming. (Underwater glide distance and speed decay)
- Mason, B. et al. (2013) - Turn performance in competitive swimming. (Wall push-off times)
- Walsh, B. et al. (2006) - Crossover arm recovery and shoulder impingement. (Entry position, crossover effects)
- Colwin, C. (1999) - Fist swimming study. (Fist drill slowdown data)
- Correia, R. et al. (2012) - University of Portsmouth research on fist drill. (~16% slowdown in well-trained swimmers)
- Counsilman, J.E. (1971) - *The Science of Swimming*. (Historical S-curve pull theory — superseded by later research)

**World Aquatics Biomechanical Reports:**
- Olympic Games and World Championships backstroke race analyses (underwater dolphin kick distances, turn times, stroke rates)

> [!warning] Quantitative Claims Disclaimer
> Many numerical claims in this document (drag percentages, time savings per race, propulsion ratios) are **estimates** from coaching literature and biomechanics research, not precise scientific measurements. Actual values vary significantly by swimmer. These figures are included to give a sense of relative importance, not as exact physics. Where possible, tiered targets replace single-number "competitive targets" to reflect different skill levels.
>
> **Research-validated claims** are cited with author names and years (e.g., Zamparo et al., 1996; Psycharakis & Sanders, 2008).
> **Coaching heuristics** are noted as such — these are practical guidelines widely used by coaches but not validated by controlled research.
> **Unverifiable claims** (e.g., exact drag reduction percentages, precise propulsion increases from specific technique changes) are flagged with ranges and caveats.
>
> For the most current research, see the sources listed above and recent publications in the *Journal of Sports Sciences*, *Sports Biomechanics*, and *Journal of Biomechanics*.
