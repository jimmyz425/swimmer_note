## Overview
The flutter kick in backstroke is a hip-driven, continuous motion that provides propulsion and helps maintain body position. A compact kick with relaxed ankles and proper rhythm is essential for both sprint and distance backstroke events.

**Difficulty:** Easy-Moderate

## Key Points to Remember
- **Kick from hips** - Same as freestyle, hip-driven motion
- **Compact amplitude** - 12-18 inches maximum; keep kick within body's "shadow"
- **Relaxed ankles** - Loose, flexible ankles for propulsion
- **Continuous rhythm** - No pauses, always kicking
- **Slight knee bend** - Not rigid, but not bicycle kick
- **Downbeat is power phase** - Whip-like motion from hip
- **Upbeat is recovery phase** - Bring foot back up efficiently

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Bicycle kicking** - Excessive knee bend, kicks from knees instead of hips. Fix: Straighten legs, engage hip flexors and glutes
- **Kick too deep** - Creates drag, sinks hips. Fix: Keep kicks shallow, near surface
- **Stiff ankles** - Reduces propulsion. Fix: Relax ankles, let toes point naturally
- **Kicking too slow** - Loses momentum. Fix: Aim for steady, rhythmic kicks
- **Wide kick** - Legs spread too far apart, increases drag. Fix: Keep legs closer together
- **Over-kicking** - Wastes energy. Fix: Slow down; aim for steady, rhythmic kicks

## Competitive Drills

#### Drill 1: Vertical Back Kicking (deep water, arms crossed at chest)
**Self-Check:** Hold vertical position with chin above water, no arm movement

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** 15-30 seconds continuous hold
> - **Intermediate:** 30-60 seconds
> - **Advanced:** 60-90 seconds
> - **Elite:** ≥90 seconds, ≥30 seconds with fists closed
>
> Self-check cue: If your chin drops below water, your kick is inefficient
>
> **Note:** These hold times are widely used as coaching benchmarks (particularly in US college and club programs) but are not validated by formal research as predictive of race performance. They are useful as a general indicator of kick strength and endurance.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Knee bend: ≤30 degrees at maximum bend (visual estimate — slight bend only, no bicycle motion). **Note:** Some knee bend is necessary and natural; the key is that the bend is driven by hip extension, not active knee flexion. Excessive bend (>45 degrees) indicates "bicycle kicking."
- Kick tempo: Continuous, steady rhythm. **Note:** The "≥130 bpm" figure is an estimate for 6-beat kick patterns. Actual kick rate varies by event (sprint vs. distance) and individual. Some elite backstrokers use 2-beat or 4-beat kick patterns in distance events with lower kick rates (~60-80 bpm for 2-beat). Kick tempo is not universally "faster than freestyle" — it depends on the swimmer's kick pattern.
- Competitive target: Ankles pointed with flexible plantar flexion. **Note:** Plantar flexion range of motion is a measurable predictor of kick efficiency. Elite swimmers typically have 50+ degrees of plantar flexion. The "35% more propulsion" claim for flexible ankles is a coaching estimate; actual improvement depends on baseline ankle stiffness.

**Competitive Impact:** Backstroke kick provides approximately **10-15% of race propulsion in distance events and up to 25-30% in sprint events**. **Research basis:** Zamparo et al. (1996) used the active drag method to isolate kick contribution, finding ranges from ~10% in distance swimming to ~30%+ in sprint swimming, with significant individual variation. **Correction:** The document previously stated "20-25%, more than any other stroke." This is inaccurate — freestyle sprint kick contributes a similar or higher percentage (some elite sprinters generate 30%+ from their kick). The backstroke kick's role is comparable to freestyle, not greater. The kick also plays a critical role in stabilizing body rotation, not just propulsion.

#### Drill 2: 6-Beat Kick Set (4x100m, arms at sides, kick only)
**Self-Check:** Time per 100m, count kicks per lap

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Kick-Only 100m, LCM)
> - **Beginner:** ≤2:30 per 100m
> - **Intermediate:** ≤2:00 per 100m
> - **Advanced:** ≤1:40 per 100m
> - **Elite:** ≤1:20 per 100m (male), ≤1:30 per 100m (female)
>
> Self-check cue: Count kicks on one leg to ensure consistent 6-beat rhythm
>
> **Note:** The ≤32 kicks per 25m target assumes ~8 kicks per leg per 25m in a 6-beat pattern, but actual kick count per lap depends heavily on stroke rate and tempo. Use kick count as a consistency measure, not an absolute target.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Kick amplitude: ≤30-40cm (12-16 inches) from highest to lowest point. **Note:** Amplitude varies by body size and leg length. The key is keeping the kick within the body's "shadow" — feet should not break the water surface significantly during horizontal swimming.
- No bicycle kick: Knees do not bend excessively (visual check for knee-driven vs. hip-driven motion)
- Competitive target: Kick originates from hips (no visible upper leg lag — thighs should initiate motion)

**Competitive Impact:** Every improvement in kick efficiency contributes to overall speed and body position. Kick quality is especially critical in sprint events where propulsion demands are highest. *The relationship "0.1m/s faster kick = ~0.7s saved per 100m" is a coaching estimate; actual time savings depend on how kick improvement translates to overall race pace and varies by event distance.*
