## Overview
The arm recovery is the phase where the arm moves through the air from exit to entry. A high elbow recovery with a relaxed hand, kept close to the body line, is most efficient. The recovery path directly determines the entry position for the next stroke.
**Difficulty: Moderate-Hard**

## Key Points to Remember
- **High elbow recovery** - Elbow leads, hand trails below elbow
- **Relaxed hand/wrist** - Hand should be loose, not rigid
- **Hand close to body** - Recovery path stays near body line, not wide swing
- **Lead with elbow** - Elbow lifts first, hand follows relaxed
- **Clear the water** - Hand exits water cleanly, doesn't drag
- **Smooth transition** - Recovery flows into next entry

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Low, wide recovery** - Hand swings wide at water level
- **Straight arm recovery** - Arm stays straight throughout recovery
- **Rigid hand** - Hand tense, fingers spread wide
- **Dragging hand** - Hand doesn't fully clear water surface
- **Throwing hand forward** - Recovery too fast, loses control of entry
- **Chicken wing** - Elbow tucked too close to body

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Finger Trail Drill** | During recovery, drag fingertips along water surface. Develops high elbow and relaxed hand. |
| **Zipper Drill** | Imagine pulling hand up along an imaginary zipper on your body side. |
| **High Elbow Pause** | At mid-recovery, pause briefly with elbow high, hand low. Feel the position. |
| **Single-Arm Recovery** | Swim with one arm, focus purely on recovery mechanics. |
| **Thumb-to-Thigh Drill** | Touch thumb to thigh during exit, then recover high elbow. |

## Competitive Drills
#### Drill 1: Finger Trail Drill (4x50m set)
**Self-Check:** Count percentage of strokes where fingertips drag along water surface during recovery

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥50% of strokes
> - **Intermediate:** ≥75% of strokes
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≥95% of strokes
>
> Self-check cue: Feel the water on your fingertips the entire recovery path — this confirms high elbow and correct hand height
>
> **Note:** These percentage targets are coaching heuristics, not research-validated benchmarks. They represent a progression from inconsistent to consistent high-elbow recovery. For self-coaching, focus on the qualitative feel (fingertips consistently touching water) rather than exact percentage counts.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Elbow height: Elbow clearly above hand at mid-recovery (visual assessment — elbow should be the highest point of the recovering arm)
- Recovery width: Hand stays close to body line (no wide outward swing)
- Competitive target: No hand drag or splashing during recovery (fingers just touch surface)

**Competitive Impact:** Wide recovery increases frontal drag and wastes energy. Keeping recovery close to the body line is more efficient, especially over longer distances. *Wide recovery can significantly increase drag — the hand moving through air outside the body's "shadow" creates additional resistance and disrupts body balance. The exact drag increase depends on how wide the swing is, but a wide recovery (hand >30cm outside body line) may add 5-10% to total race drag.*

#### Drill 2: Thumb-to-Thigh Drill (4x100m set)
**Self-Check:** Count percentage of strokes where your thumb touches your thigh on exit

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥60% of strokes complete full pull to hip
> - **Intermediate:** ≥80% of strokes
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≥95% of strokes
>
> Self-check cue: Focus on finishing each pull completely before starting recovery — feel the hand pass your hip
>
> **Note:** These targets are coaching heuristics for pull completion consistency. The key metric is whether the hand exits at hip level (not early), which can be assessed qualitatively through video or feel for water.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Exit point: Hand exits at or near hip level (no early exit before hip, no excessive push past hip)
- Recovery quality: Relaxed hand/wrist during recovery (visible loose fingers, no fist clenching)
- Competitive target: Quick, controlled recovery transition (recovery should feel lighter and faster than the pull phase)

**Competitive Impact:** Early exit (hand leaving water before reaching hip) loses a portion of pull propulsion. Completing the full pull ensures you capture all available propulsion from each stroke. **Note:** The late pull phase (from hip-level to exit) contributes approximately 10-15% of total pull propulsion (Schleihauf, 1979). Exiting 10-15cm before the hip may lose this contribution.
