## Overview
The catch is the initial phase where the hand anchors on the water immediately after entry. A high-elbow, early vertical forearm (EVF) position creates maximum leverage by using the entire forearm as a propulsive surface. This is one of the most impactful technique improvements a swimmer can make.

**Difficulty:** Moderate-Hard

## Key Points to Remember
- **Catch immediately after entry** - No pause between entry and catch
- **Hand rotates to catch position** - Palm faces outward briefly
- **Bend elbow to catch** - Creates leverage; high elbow above hand during catch
- **Feel water pressure** - Hand anchors on water
- **High elbow** - Elbow above hand during catch (same EVF position as freestyle)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Late catch** - Pause after entry before catching
- **Straight arm catch** - No bend, poor leverage
- **Dropped elbow** - Elbow below hand
- **Pressing down** - Pushes water down, not back
- **No catch feel** - Hand slips through water

## Specific Drills

| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Sculling on Back** | Develop feel for water pressure |
| **Fist Drill Backstroke** | Closed fists, use forearm for catch |
| **Catch Pause** | Pause at catch position, feel it |
| **Single-Arm Catch** | Focus on catch mechanics alone |
| **Paddle Swimming** | Use paddles to feel catch pressure |

## Competitive Metrics

#### Drill 1: Fist Backstroke Drill (4x100m set, swim with closed fists)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between fist swimming vs normal swimming

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Good:** ≤20% time increase vs normal swimming (shows you're using forearm)
> - **Very Good:** ≤15% time increase
> - **Advanced:** ≤10-12% time increase
> - **Elite:** ≤8-10% time increase
>
> Self-check cue: Feel pressure on your forearms, not just your knuckles
>
> **Research basis:** Studies on fist drill slowdown report ~15-20% for recreational to competitive swimmers (Colwin, 1999; Correia et al., 2012). The ≤7% target in the original document was unrealistically low. Even elite swimmers typically see 8-10% slowdown without hands. The tiered targets above are consistent with fist drill research.

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Forearm angle: Forearm approaches vertical during catch phase (visual assessment — forearm should look like a paddle catching water). **Note:** The "75-85 degrees within 0.2s of entry" timing requires high-speed underwater video. For practical self-coaching, assess qualitatively.
- No dropped elbow: Elbow stays **above** hand during catch phase. **Correction:** The original stated "elbow stays below hand (backstroke opposite of freestyle)." This is **incorrect and contradictory** — backstroke uses the SAME high-elbow catch position as freestyle, with the elbow above the hand. Both strokes benefit from early vertical forearm (EVF) positioning.
- Competitive target: Hand/forearm presses backward, not downward (water should be pushed back, not up or down)

**Competitive Impact:** Proper backstroke catch (high elbow, vertical forearm) significantly increases pull propulsion compared to a dropped elbow catch, because it uses the entire forearm as a propulsive surface. **Correction:** The claim that it "doubles pull propulsion" is an exaggeration. Research suggests EVF can increase propulsive force by 20-50% over a poor dropped-elbow catch, with the largest gains seen in swimmers who previously had severe elbow drop. *This is one of the most impactful technique improvements a swimmer can make.*

#### Drill 2: Back Sculling (1 minute set, no kick)
**Self-Check:** Distance traveled forward in 1 minute of back sculling

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥8m per minute
> - **Intermediate:** ≥12m per minute
> - **Advanced:** ≥18m per minute
> - **Elite:** ≥25m per minute
>
> Self-check cue: Keep elbows relatively low, scull with hands/forearms. Feel the water pressure on your palms and forearms.
>
> **Note:** These distance benchmarks are coaching guidelines, not research-validated standards. Sculling speed depends on pool conditions, technique quality, and whether performed continuously or with pauses. The key value is developing water feel and catch position awareness.

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Scull angle: Forearms change angle each scull motion (visible change in hand/forearm orientation)
- Upper arm stability: Upper arms stay relatively stable, only forearms/hands move actively
- Competitive target: Consistent forward movement (no bouncing or backward movement)

**Competitive Impact:** Strong sculling ability indicates good water feel and catch efficiency. **Correction:** The claim "Every 10% increase in catch efficiency = ~2.5% faster swim speed" is an unverifiable coaching estimate. There is no direct linear relationship between catch efficiency improvements and swim speed that has been validated by research. Improvements in catch mechanics do translate to better propulsion, but the exact ratio is individual-dependent.
