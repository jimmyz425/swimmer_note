## Overview
The pull/push phase is where most propulsion is generated in backstroke. A relatively straight hand path (with minor lateral adjustments of 5-15cm) combined with high elbow and acceleration through to the hip finish creates maximum forward thrust. Pull contributes approximately 70-85% of race propulsion in sprint events.

**Difficulty:** Moderate-Hard

## Key Points to Remember
- **Pull relatively straight back** - Hand path is relatively straight with minor lateral adjustments (5-15cm), not exaggerated S-curve
- **Bend elbow during pull** - 90-100 degrees ideal at mid-pull; high elbow throughout
- **Accelerate through pull** - Speed increases from catch to finish
- **Finish at hip** - Exit hand at hip level, not past it; pushing past hip causes unnecessary delay in recovery

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Straight arm pull** - No leverage, inefficient
- **Pulling too wide** - Hand outside shoulder line
- **Not finishing** - Stroke incomplete at hip
- **Pulling past hip** - Over-finishing causes delay
- **Slow pull** - No acceleration

## Competitive Metrics

#### Drill 1: Single-Arm Pull (4x50m per arm, pull buoy only, no kick)
**Self-Check:** Time per 50m single-arm swimming

> [!note] Targets
> - Competitive target: ≤2x your normal 50m backstroke time. **Note:** The ≤1.8x target is aggressive — single-arm swimming removes half your propulsion and eliminates the stabilizing effect of the opposite arm. Many swimmers achieve 1.8-2.5x. Use this as a personal baseline rather than an absolute standard.
> - Self-check cue: Feel your lats (back muscles) doing the work, not your triceps

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Pull path: Hand travels relatively straight back parallel to body line, finishing at thigh. **Note:** Minor lateral deviations (5-15cm) are normal and can aid hand stability.
- Exit point: Hand exits at or near hip level (not early, not excessively past hip)
- Competitive target: Pull accelerates from catch to finish (hand moving faster at exit than at entry). **Note:** The "≥35% hand speed increase" requires motion-tracking software to measure precisely. For practical self-coaching, focus on the *feeling* of acceleration.

**Competitive Impact:** Modern 3D motion analysis research supports a relatively straight pull path over the exaggerated S-curve. **Correction:** The "30% more efficient" claim is a coaching estimate without direct research backing. Schleihauf's original 3D analysis showed elite swimmers' hand paths have modest lateral deviations (5-15cm from a straight line), not large S-curves. The key finding is that dramatic S-shapes are inefficient; a relatively straight path with minor lateral adjustments is optimal. *Exact efficiency gains depend on the swimmer's baseline technique.*

#### Drill 2: Pull Buoy Only Swimming (4x100m, no kick allowed)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between pull buoy swimming vs normal swimming

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≤15% time increase
> - **Intermediate:** ≤10% time increase
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≤5-6% time increase
>
> Self-check cue: Maintain same stroke tempo as normal swimming. Don't slow your stroke rate to compensate for lost kick.
>
> **Note:** These percentages represent *time increase* with a pull buoy (which eliminates kick but adds buoyancy). The pull buoy reduces leg drag by providing flotation, so the time increase from eliminating kick is less than the kick's propulsion contribution would suggest.

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- High elbow maintained: Elbow stays **above** wrist throughout entire pull phase. **Correction:** The original stated "low elbow" — this is incorrect. Backstroke benefits from the same high-elbow pull position as freestyle, which maximizes propulsive surface area.
- No arm crossing: Hand never crosses body center line during pull
- Competitive target: Comparable pull mechanics on both left and right sides

**Competitive Impact:** Pull efficiency is the single largest determinant of backstroke sprint speed. Pull contributes approximately 70-85% of race propulsion in sprint events (kick 15-30%) and 85-90% in distance events (kick 10-15%). These figures are based on the active drag measurement method (Zamparo et al., 1996) and have been supported by subsequent studies, though individual variation is significant.
