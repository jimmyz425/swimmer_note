## Overview

The whip kick is widely considered the most challenging component of breaststroke, accounting for approximately 40% of the learning challenge. It is also the primary propulsion source. The kick involves a specific sequence: heels recover toward the buttocks (not knees to chest), feet turn outward, and a circular whip motion generates thrust culminating in a powerful snap as the feet come together. Proper ankle flexibility is essential, and the kick must comply with World Aquatics rules requiring outward foot position.

**Difficulty:** Hardest (Most Challenging)

## Key Points to Remember

- **Recovery: heels toward buttocks (not knees to chest)** - keep knees close together (shoulder-width or narrower), flex knees to approximately 90-130 degrees at maximum bend
- **Catch: turn feet outward (dorsiflex and evert)** - feet should turn outward significantly, ideally approaching 90 degrees of external rotation; most competitive swimmers achieve 45-75 degrees
- **Propulsive phase: kick backward and outward in circular motion** - drive from hips through thighs, the insweep of feet creates most thrust
- **Snap phase: whip feet together quickly** - this is where most power is generated, finish with legs straight and streamlined
- **Focus on the whip snap at the end** - kicking without the final squeeze loses most propulsion
- **Feet MUST be turned outward during propulsive phase** - required by World Aquatics rules (SW 7.5); without outward foot position, you get a scissor kick which is illegal
- **Ankle flexibility exercises regularly** - ignoring ankle mobility limits kick effectiveness

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Knees to chest (frog kick style)** - creates massive drag, the most common error in breaststroke kick
- **Knees going wide** - knees wider than hip-width dramatically increases drag
- **Feet staying pointed straight** - without outward foot turn, no thrust is generated
- **Kicking without the final squeeze** - missing the whip snap loses the most propulsive moment

## Specific Drills

| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Wall Kick** | Hold pool edge, practice whip kick stationary. 3 sets of 20 kicks. |
| **Kick on Back** | Lie on back, arms at sides, practice whip kick. Watch knees stay close. 4x25m. |
| **Kick with Board** | Hold kickboard, focus purely on kick technique. 4x25m with rest. |
| **Vertical Kick** | Tread water vertically using only whip kick. Hands out of water. 3 sets of 30 sec. |
| **Single-Leg Kick** | Hold wall, practice kick with one leg. 10 kicks per leg. Identifies asymmetries. |
| **Ankle Flexibility Exercises** | Daily ankle mobility work. Sit on heels with feet turned outward. |

## Competitive Metrics

#### Drill 1: Kick Only Drill (4x50m, kick board, no pull)

**Self-Check:** Time per 50m kick, kick count per lap

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Kick-Only 50m — Kick Board, No Pull)
> - **Beginner:** ≤60 seconds per 50m (LCM), ≤20 kicks per 25m
> - **Intermediate:** ≤50 seconds per 50m, ≤16 kicks per 25m
> - **Advanced:** ≤45 seconds per 50m, ≤14 kicks per 25m
> - **Elite:** ≤40 seconds per 50m, ≤12 kicks per 25m
>
> Self-check cue: Feel the "snap" of your feet at the end of the kick, not just pushing water back
>
> **Note:** Kick-only times vary widely based on kick strength. The kick count per 25m reflects efficiency — fewer, more powerful kicks are better than many small, ineffective ones.

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Heel distance: Heels come within **5-15cm** of buttocks during recovery phase. *(Note: the "10cm" target is a reasonable average; closer heel recovery creates a more compact, less draggy position, but exact distance varies with swimmer flexibility.)*
- Foot rotation: Feet turn out significantly during the whip phase (ideally approaching 90 degrees of external rotation; **45-75 degrees** is the typical competitive range; full 90 degrees requires exceptional ankle flexibility)
- Competitive target: Kick amplitude **≤60cm** (measured from widest point of foot path). Wide kicking creates drag. *(Note: this requires overhead/underwater video with a reference scale for accurate measurement.)*

**Competitive Impact:** Breaststroke kick is the primary propulsion source. Coaching literature commonly cites **60-70% contribution**, though recent biomechanical studies using 3D motion analysis and force measurements suggest a range of **50-60% for elite sprinters** and potentially lower for distance events. The kick's outsweep/insweep phase generates thrust through both lift and drag forces, with the final "whip snap" being the most propulsive moment. *(Note: exact percentages vary by study methodology and swimmer.)*

#### Drill 2: Vertical Breaststroke Kick (deep water, arms crossed at chest)

**Self-Check:** Hold vertical position with chin above water, no arm movement

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Vertical Kick Hold Duration — Arms Crossed)
> - **Beginner:** 10-15 seconds continuous hold with chin above water
> - **Intermediate:** 15-20 seconds
> - **Advanced:** 20-30 seconds
> - **Elite:** ≥30 seconds with arms crossed; ≥10 seconds with fists closed
>
> Self-check cue: If your chin drops below water, your kick is inefficient (you're not generating enough upward propulsion)
>
> **Note:** Vertical kick is one of the best indicators of kick power and endurance. The fists-closed benchmark is an advanced variation that further tests ankle flexibility and kick efficiency.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Knee width: Knees stay within **hip-width or shoulder-width** during kick (narrow knees = less drag). *(Note: "hip-width" is a more accurate target than "shoulder-width" since hips are narrower; some coaching sources use shoulder-width as an upper limit.)*
- Whip timing: Feet snap outward then inward in one continuous motion
- Competitive target: Ankles are flexible enough to create a "paddle" effect during the snap

**Competitive Impact:** Kick efficiency has the largest impact on breaststroke speed of any single component. The commonly cited formula "every 10% kick efficiency increase = 4% faster speed" is a coaching approximation. In practice, kick improvements yield the greatest speed gains because the kick is both the primary propulsion source and the key determinant of body position during the glide.
