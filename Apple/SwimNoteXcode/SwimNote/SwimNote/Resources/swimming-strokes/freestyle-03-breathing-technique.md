## Overview
Proper breathing technique is essential for maintaining body position and rhythm. The key is to rotate the body to bring the mouth to air rather than lifting the head, which causes the hips to drop. Continuous underwater exhalation prevents breath-holding tension.
**Difficulty: Moderate**

## Key Points to Remember
- **Rotate, don't lift** - Roll your body to breathe, never lift your head straight up
- **One goggle in water** - When breathing, keep one eye/goggle below waterline
- **Continuous underwater exhalation** - Exhale steadily while face is submerged
- **Timing with arm recovery** - Breathe when your arm is recovering/exiting water
- **Bilateral breathing** - Practice breathing on both sides (every 3 strokes) for balance
- **Look sideways, not up** - Mouth opens to the side, not toward ceiling

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Lifting head too high** - Causes hips to sink immediately
- **Holding breath underwater** - Creates tension, wastes energy
- **Late timing** - Trying to breathe too late in stroke cycle
- **Over-rotating** - Turning head too far (looking at ceiling/sky)
- **Looking forward while breathing** - Disrupts body alignment
- **Breathing every stroke** - Can break rhythm and rotation balance

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Sink Downs** | Sink underwater, exhale continuously through nose/mouth. Learn to exhale naturally. |
| **Side Kick Breathing** | Kick on side with face down, rotate slightly to breathe, return face down. Practice head rotation without lifting. |
| **6-1-6 Breathing Drill** | Kick 6 times on side (face down), rotate to breathe during 1 stroke, return to other side. Perfect timing practice. *See also [[swimming-strokes/freestyle-02-flutter-kick]] and [[swimming-strokes/freestyle-04-body-rotation]] sections for this drill.* |
| **One Goggle Drill** | Focus on keeping exactly one goggle in water when breathing. |
| **Bilateral Pattern Drill** | Swim breathing every 3 strokes (left-right-left) consistently. |

## Competitive Metrics
#### Drill 1: One Goggle Drill (200m continuous)
**Self-Check:** Count percentage of breaths where you feel one goggle still submerged

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥70% of breaths maintain one-goggle position
> - **Intermediate:** ≥85% of breaths
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≥95% of breaths
>
> Self-check cue: In an indoor pool, if you see the ceiling when breathing, you lifted too high. In an outdoor pool, if you see the sky above the horizon line, you lifted too high.
>
> **Note:** These percentages are coaching heuristics. Counting breath accuracy percentages during swimming is challenging without video review. For practical self-coaching, use the "see the ceiling" self-check and periodic video review to assess breathing technique.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Head movement: Visible head rotation only, no vertical lift above neutral position (assess visually — head should rotate, not rise)
- Competitive target: Breathing does not disrupt stroke rhythm (stroke count and tempo remain consistent between breathing and non-breathing strokes)

> [!tip] Practical Proxy Metric
> Instead of trying to measure breathing time duration (requires high-speed video analysis), use **stroke count consistency**: if your stroke count over 50m is the same whether you breathe every 2, 3, or 4 strokes, your breathing technique is efficient.

**Competitive Impact:** Lifting the head disrupts body alignment and causes the hips to drop. The commonly cited "lift head ~5cm, drop hips ~10cm" ratio is a coaching heuristic (not a fixed 2:1 lever law) — the actual relationship varies by body proportions, torso length, and individual buoyancy distribution. Research by Yanai and Caldwell (1999) confirms the head-hip connection through the body's center of mass and center of buoyancy. The resulting drag increase (estimated 15-25% from poor body position based on CFD studies such as Bixler & Rieweg, 1998; Bixler et al., 2007) can cost approximately 1s per 100m for moderate head lift. *Exact values vary by body type and baseline technique. The 15-25% drag increase represents total frontal drag increase from severe body position errors (dropped hips + head lift combined), not from head lift alone.*

#### Drill 2: Bilateral Breathing Set (4x100m, alternate breathing side each 100m)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between breathing left vs right side

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≤5% time difference between sides
> - **Intermediate:** ≤3% time difference
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≤2% time difference (note: measuring ≤2% reliably requires electronic timing; hand-timed results have ±0.3s variability)
>
> Self-check cue: Count strokes per 50m on each side to compare efficiency
>
> **Note:** These symmetry targets are coaching guidelines. A time difference of ≤2% over 100m equals ≤0.2s for a 1:00 swimmer, which is at the limit of what hand timing can resolve. Stroke count comparison (e.g., same stroke count breathing left vs. right over 50m) is a more practical self-check for most swimmers.

**Video Check (underwater footage, if available):**
- Exhalation quality: No visible breath-holding signs (tension in neck/shoulders, bubbles held then released in bursts). Aim for steady, continuous bubble stream while face is submerged.
- Symmetry: Same rotation angle and head position for left and right breaths (visual assessment)
- Competitive target: No obvious stroke length difference between breathing and non-breathing strokes

**Competitive Impact:** Symmetrical breathing prevents muscle imbalances and one-sided drag loss. Asymmetrical breathing can also cause lane-line collisions in open water.
