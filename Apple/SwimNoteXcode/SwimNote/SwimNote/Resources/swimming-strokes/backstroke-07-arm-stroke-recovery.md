## Overview
The arm recovery in backstroke uses a bent-arm motion where the elbow leads and the hand trails below. The thumb exits the water first, the hand rotates during recovery, and the pinky leads entry. A relaxed recovery path close to the body line is efficient and conserves energy.

**Difficulty:** Moderate

## Key Points to Remember
- **Bent-arm recovery** - Elbow leads, hand trails below elbow (NOT straight arm). Modern backstroke technique uses bent-elbow recovery where the elbow lifts first and hand trails below
- **Thumb exits, pinky enters** - Thumb leads exit from water, hand rotates during recovery, pinky leads entry
- **Arm close to body** - Not wide swing; recovery path is an arc close to the body
- **Relaxed arm** - Not rigid or tense
- **Smooth rotation** - Hand rotates during recovery (thumb to pinky)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Straight arm recovery** - Arm straight during recovery wastes energy and increases drag (this is the OLD technique; modern technique uses bent elbow)
- **Wide recovery** - Arm swings too far from body
- **Tense arm** - Rigid, wastes energy
- **No rotation** - Hand doesn't rotate during recovery
- **Dragging hand** - Hand doesn't fully exit

## Specific Drills

| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Thumb Exit Focus** | Focus on thumb exiting water first |
| **High Elbow Recovery** | Practice lifting elbow first during recovery, hand trailing below elbow. Feel the bent-arm recovery position. |
| **Rotation Drill** | Practice thumb-to-pinky hand rotation |
| **Relaxed Recovery** | Focus on loose, easy arm motion |
| **Single-Arm Recovery** | Isolate recovery mechanics |

## Competitive Metrics

#### Drill 1: Thumb Drag Drill / High Elbow Recovery (4x50m set, drag thumb along water surface during recovery)
**Self-Check:** Count percentage of strokes where your thumb drags along water surface during recovery

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥50% of strokes
> - **Intermediate:** ≥75% of strokes
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≥95% of strokes
>
> Self-check cue: Feel the water on your thumb the entire recovery path — this confirms high elbow and correct hand height
>
> **Note:** These percentage targets are coaching heuristics, not research-validated benchmarks. For self-coaching, focus on the qualitative feel rather than exact percentage counts.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Recovery path: Arm travels close to body line, not wide. **Note:** The recovery path is an arc close to the body, not strictly "perpendicular to body."
- Bent arm: Elbow leads recovery, hand trails below. **Correction:** The original stated "full arm extension during recovery, no elbow bend." This is incorrect — elite backstrokers use bent-arm recovery with the elbow clearly above the hand at mid-recovery.
- Competitive target: Hand enters water at approximately shoulder line, no wide swing

**Competitive Impact:** Wide recovery increases frontal drag and wastes energy. *The "25% more frontal drag" figure is a coaching estimate; actual drag increase depends on how wide the swing is. Keeping recovery close to the body line is more efficient, especially over longer distances.*

#### Drill 2: Little Finger Exit Drill (4x100m set)
**Self-Check:** Count percentage of strokes where your little finger exits the water first

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥60% of strokes complete full pull to hip
> - **Intermediate:** ≥80% of strokes
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≥95% of strokes
>
> Self-check cue: Focus on finishing each pull completely before starting recovery — feel the hand pass your hip
>
> **Note:** These targets are coaching heuristics for pull completion consistency. The key metric is whether the hand exits at hip level (not early).

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Exit point: Hand exits at or near hip level (no early exit before hip, no excessive push past hip)
- Recovery quality: Relaxed hand/wrist during recovery (visible loose fingers, no fist clenching). **Note:** The "1.3x faster than pull phase" recovery speed ratio is a coaching estimate; actual speed ratios vary by swimmer and event.
- Competitive target: Quick, controlled recovery transition

**Competitive Impact:** Early exit (hand leaving water before reaching hip) loses a portion of pull propulsion. Completing the full pull ensures you capture all available propulsion from each stroke. *The late pull phase (from hip-level to exit) contributes approximately 10-15% of total pull propulsion. Exiting 10-15cm before the hip may lose this contribution.*
