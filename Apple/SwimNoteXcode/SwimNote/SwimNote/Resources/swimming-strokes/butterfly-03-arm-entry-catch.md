## Overview
The arm entry and catch phase sets up the entire underwater pull. Hands enter shoulder-width apart, fingertips first, establishing a high elbow catch position quickly. Proper entry prevents shoulder stress and maximizes the water anchoring that drives propulsion.
**Difficulty: Moderate**

## Key Points to Remember
- Enter water shoulder-width apart, slightly in front of shoulders
- Hands enter fingertips first, then wrists, then forearms
- Reach forward and slightly outward after entry
- Establish high elbow position quickly (catch)
- Press palms outward and slightly down to catch water
- Bend elbows to approximately 90 degrees during catch

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Entering too wide (shoulder stress, weak catch)
- Entering too narrow (reduced leverage)
- Slapping water with flat hands
- Dropped elbows during catch
- Overreaching causing loss of momentum
- Thumbs entering first (rotator cuff stress)

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Single-Arm Butterfly** | Swim with one arm extended forward, other arm doing complete stroke. Switch arms each 25m. |
| **Catch-Up Drill** | Touch hands together in front before each stroke. Emphasizes proper entry position and timing. |
| **Sculling Drill** | Arms extended forward, practice small sculling motions to feel water pressure on hands. |
| **Fist Swimming** | Swim with closed fists to emphasize forearm catch and feel for water. |

## Competitive Drills
#### Drill 1: Catch Only Drill (4x50m, dolphin kick only, focus on catch phase, no full pull)
**Self-Check:** Distance per stroke (stroke length) during catch drills

> [!tip] Tiered Catch Distance Per Stroke
> | Level | Distance Per Stroke | Notes |
> |-------|--------------------|-------|
> | **Beginner** | 1.0-1.3m | Learning catch mechanics; focus on high elbow |
> | **Intermediate** | 1.3-1.6m | Consistent catch setup; improving feel for water |
> | **Advanced** | 1.6-1.9m | Strong catch anchoring; good distance maintenance |
> | **Elite** | 1.9-2.2m | Maximum catch efficiency; elite distance per stroke |

- Self-check cue: Feel your hands "anchor" in the water immediately after entry, no slipping

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Entry position: Hands enter shoulder width apart, little finger first, minimal splash
- Catch angle: Forearms orient to a near-vertical position (high elbow catch) during the catch phase. Catch setup time varies with stroke rate -- faster rates mean shorter catch setup.
- Elbows stay above wrists during catch (no dropped elbows)

**Competitive Impact:** Proper catch technique with high elbow position significantly increases pull propulsion compared to a dropped elbow catch. Research on catch mechanics shows that maintaining an effective catch angle (forearm vertical early) can improve propulsion efficiency substantially -- the "doubles" claim is a coaching heuristic; the actual improvement varies by swimmer but is consistently significant.

#### Drill 2: Fist Butterfly Drill (4x100m set, swim with closed fists)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between fist swimming vs normal swimming

> [!tip] Tiered Fist Drill Time Increase
> | Level | Time Increase vs Normal | Interpretation |
> |-------|----------------------|----------------|
> | **Beginner** | 25-35% | Heavy reliance on hand surface; developing forearm catch |
> | **Intermediate** | 15-25% | Improving forearm engagement; some hand reliance |
> | **Advanced** | 10-15% | Effective forearm catch; minimal hand dependency |
> | **Elite** | 8-12% | Excellent forearm propulsion; nearly identical catch mechanics |
>
> *Note: Research shows fists reduce propulsive surface area by approximately 15-20%. Increases >20% indicate over-reliance on hand surface area for propulsion.*

- Self-check cue: Feel pressure on your forearms, not just your knuckles, during the catch

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- No over-sweeping: Hands never go wider than shoulder width during catch
- No cross-over: Hands never cross body center line during entry
- Catch phase mechanics identical to open-hand swimming (same forearm angle and timing)

**Competitive Impact:** Improved catch efficiency directly translates to faster butterfly speed. The claim that every 10% increase in catch efficiency equals ~2.5% faster speed is a rough coaching estimate; the actual relationship is non-linear and depends on overall technique quality.
