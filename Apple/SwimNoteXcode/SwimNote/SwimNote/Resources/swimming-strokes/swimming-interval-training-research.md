## 1. Overview: Why Interval Training in Swimming

Interval training is the cornerstone of competitive swimming preparation. Unlike continuous training, intervals allow swimmers to accumulate high-quality volume at specific intensities while managing fatigue through structured rest periods. The fundamental principle is that the **rest interval** between repetitions determines which energy system is stressed and what adaptations occur.

Swimming presents unique challenges for interval training that differ from land-based sports:

- **Buoyancy and drag dynamics**: Active drag increases with the square of velocity, meaning small pace changes produce large energy cost differences (Toussaint & Beek, 1992; Narita et al., 2018).
- **Breathing constraints**: Unlike running or cycling, swimmers cannot breathe freely, affecting oxygen uptake and heart rate response.
- **Pool environment**: Interval timing depends on pool length, turn frequency, and lane availability.
- **Heart rate monitoring limitations**: Water temperature, body position, and hydrostatic pressure depress heart rate by approximately 10-17 bpm compared to land-based exercise at the same oxygen consumption (Chatard et al., 1992).

> **Key principle**: The interval (send-off time) is the primary coaching lever. It simultaneously controls work intensity, rest duration, and total session volume. Getting intervals right is the single most important skill in swim coaching.

## 2. Training Zones by Purpose

### 2.1 Aerobic Endurance / Base Building (Zone 1-2)

**Purpose:** Build mitochondrial density, capillary networks, stroke efficiency, and aerobic enzyme activity. This is the foundation upon which all other training is built.

#### Interval Distance Selection


| Distance | Use Case                                                | Notes                     |
| -------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| 200-400m | Primary aerobic intervals for distance swimmers (800m+) | 4-8 repetitions typical   |
| 100-200m | Standard aerobic intervals for mid-distance (200-400m)  | 8-16 repetitions typical  |
| 50-100m  | Aerobic intervals for sprinters, beginners              | 12-24 repetitions typical |


#### Rest Interval

- **Fixed rest**: 15-30 seconds between repetitions (swimming-based recovery with pull buoy or easy kick)
- **Percentage-based**: Rest = 10-20% of work time
- **Heart rate-based**: Wait until HR drops to 120-130 bpm before next repetition (where measurable)

#### Intensity Targets


| Metric               | Target                                                                              |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Pace relative to CSS | CSS pace + 5-10 seconds per 100m (Zone 1); CSS pace + 2-5 seconds per 100m (Zone 2) |
| Heart rate zone      | 60-75% HRmax (Zone 1); 75-82% HRmax (Zone 2)                                        |
| RPE (6-20 Borg)      | 10-12 (Zone 1); 12-14 (Zone 2)                                                      |
| RPE (1-10 scale)     | 2-3 (Zone 1); 3-4 (Zone 2)                                                          |
| Lactate              | < 2.0 mmol/L                                                                        |
| Perceived effort     | Conversational; can maintain technique for entire set                               |


#### Volume Recommendations


| Swimmer Level | Per Set      | Per Session (total aerobic) | Weekly Volume |
| ------------- | ------------ | --------------------------- | ------------- |
| Beginner      | 800-1,500m   | 1,500-2,500m                | 3-5 km        |
| Intermediate  | 1,500-3,000m | 3,000-5,000m                | 15-25 km      |
| Advanced      | 3,000-5,000m | 5,000-8,000m                | 30-50 km      |
| Elite         | 4,000-8,000m | 8,000-12,000m               | 50-80 km      |


> **Coaching note:** Elite distance swimmers at altitude training camps (e.g., Flagstaff, Sierra Nevada) may exceed 80 km/week, but this volume is built over years. Rapid volume increases are a primary risk factor for shoulder overuse injuries.

#### Sample Sets

```
Aerobic Base - Intermediate/Advanced
  10 x 200m freestyle on 3:00-3:15 (CSS pace + 5s/100m)
  Rest: 20s between reps (built into send-off)
  Focus: Consistent pacing, strong technique maintenance

Aerobic Endurance - Distance Focus
  5 x 400m freestyle on 5:30-6:00
  Descend 1-3, 4-5 even
  Equipment: Pull buoy on odd reps, swim on even reps

Aerobic Base - Sprinters
  16 x 100m on 1:30-1:45
  Easy/moderate effort, focus on technique
  4 rounds of 4 with 30s rest between rounds
```

### 2.2 Aerobic Threshold (AeT) / Tempo (Zone 3)

**Purpose:** Develop the ability to sustain moderately hard efforts. This is the pace at which a swimmer can hold a conversation but with effort -- the transition point where lactate begins to rise above baseline but has not yet accumulated rapidly.

#### Interval Distance Selection


| Distance | Use Case                             |
| -------- | ------------------------------------ |
| 100-300m | Primary tempo intervals              |
| 50-100m  | Tempo intervals for sprinters        |
| 400-500m | Extended tempo for distance swimmers |


#### Rest Interval

- **Fixed rest**: 15-20 seconds between repetitions
- **Percentage-based**: Rest = 10-15% of work time
- **Send-off method**: Set send-offs that allow completion of all reps at target pace with 10-20s rest

#### Intensity Targets


| Metric               | Target                                                         |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Pace relative to CSS | CSS pace to CSS pace + 2s per 100m                             |
| Heart rate zone      | 82-88% HRmax                                                   |
| RPE (6-20 Borg)      | 14-15                                                          |
| RPE (1-10 scale)     | 5-6                                                            |
| Lactate              | 2.0-2.5 mmol/L (aerobic threshold)                             |
| Perceived effort     | "Comfortably hard"; can sustain for 20-40 minutes continuously |


#### Volume Recommendations


| Swimmer Level | Per Set      | Notes                                               |
| ------------- | ------------ | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Beginner      | 400-800m     | Focus on maintaining form at slightly elevated pace |
| Intermediate  | 1,000-2,000m | Can be split into 2-3 sets within a session         |
| Advanced      | 2,000-3,500m | Core tempo work, often combined with aerobic sets   |
| Elite         | 3,000-5,000m | Significant portion of training at this intensity   |


#### Sample Sets

```
Tempo - Threshold Bridge Set
  3 x (8 x 100m) on 1:20-1:30
  Pace: CSS pace (even splits)
  Rest: 30s between each block of 8
  Focus: Holding exact target pace every repetition

Tempo - Descending Build
  6 x 200m on 2:45-3:00
  Build pace each 50m within the 200
  Focus: Learning to accelerate while maintaining efficiency
```

### 2.3 Lactate Threshold / Anaerobic Threshold (Zone 4)

**Purpose:** Raise the highest pace a swimmer can sustain without lactate accumulation. This is arguably the most important training zone for events from 200m to 1500m, as it directly determines race pace sustainability.

#### The Science

At the lactate threshold (LT), blood lactate concentration rises sharply above baseline (typically 3.5-4.0 mmol/L, though individual variation is significant). Training at or near this intensity:

- Increases lactate transport capacity (MCT1/MCT4 transporters)
- Improves lactate clearance and utilization as fuel
- Raises the pace at which threshold occurs
- Enhances buffering capacity

#### Interval Distance Selection


| Distance | Use Case                   | Notes                                                  |
| -------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| 100-200m | Primary LT intervals       | Most common for 200m-400m specialists                  |
| 200-400m | Extended LT intervals      | Best for 400m-1500m specialists                        |
| 50-100m  | LT intervals for sprinters | Shorter reps at threshold pace for 50-100m specialists |


#### Rest Interval

- **Fixed rest**: 15-30 seconds (critical: too much rest reduces lactate stress; too little causes pace to drop below threshold)
- **Percentage-based**: Rest = 5-15% of work time
- **Lactate-guided**: Target blood lactate of 3.5-5.0 mmol/L post-set

#### Intensity Targets


| Metric               | Target                                                                   |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Pace relative to CSS | CSS pace to CSS pace - 2s per 100m (slightly faster than CSS)            |
| Heart rate zone      | 88-92% HRmax                                                             |
| RPE (6-20 Borg)      | 15-17                                                                    |
| RPE (1-10 scale)     | 7-8                                                                      |
| Lactate              | 3.5-5.0 mmol/L (individual lactate threshold)                            |
| Perceived effort     | "Hard"; can sustain for approximately 20-40 minutes (1,500-3,000m total) |


#### Volume Recommendations


| Swimmer Level | Per Set      | Per Session  | Notes                                              |
| ------------- | ------------ | ------------ | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Beginner      | 400-800m     | 800-1,200m   | Introduce gradually; 1-2 sessions per week max     |
| Intermediate  | 1,000-2,000m | 1,500-3,000m | Core threshold work; 2-3 sessions per week         |
| Advanced      | 2,000-3,500m | 3,000-5,000m | Significant threshold focus; 3-4 sessions per week |
| Elite         | 3,000-5,000m | 5,000-7,000m | Multiple threshold sessions per week               |


#### Sample Sets

```
Lactate Threshold - Classic Set
  10 x 100m on 1:10-1:20 (pace: 5-10s faster than CSS pace per 100m)
  Rest: 10-20s built into send-off
  Focus: Holding identical pace on every repetition; if pace drops, intervals are too fast

Lactate Threshold - Distance Focus
  6 x 200m on 2:30-2:45 (pace: at or slightly faster than CSS pace)
  Rest: 15-25s built into send-off
  Focus: Negative split each 200 (second 100 faster than first)

Lactate Threshold - Broken Distance
  3 x (4 x 100m + 1 x 200m)
  100s on 1:15 at threshold pace
  200s on 2:40 at threshold pace
  Rest: 30s between groups
  Total: 1,800m at threshold intensity
```

> **Coaching note:** The hallmark of effective threshold training is **consistency**. If a swimmer's times drop significantly after the third or fourth repetition, the send-offs are too fast. The goal is to stress the lactate system without crossing into VO2max or anaerobic territory.

### 2.4 VO2max / Maximum Aerobic Power (Zone 5)

**Purpose:** Develop maximal oxygen uptake capacity. This is the highest intensity at which aerobic energy production can meet demand. Training here increases the ceiling of aerobic power, which benefits all events from 100m upward.

#### The Science

VO2max training targets the central cardiovascular system:

- Increases maximal stroke volume and cardiac output
- Improves oxygen extraction at the muscle level
- Enhances mitochondrial enzyme activity
- Raises the absolute aerobic power ceiling

Research shows that intervals of 2-8 minutes at 90-100% of vVO2max (velocity at VO2max) are most effective for improving VO2max (Billat et al., 1999; Midgley et al., 2006). In swimming, this typically translates to 50-400m repetitions.

#### Interval Distance Selection


| Distance | Use Case                  | Notes                                              |
| -------- | ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| 50-100m  | Primary VO2max intervals  | 6-12 repetitions; common for 100m-400m specialists |
| 100-200m | Extended VO2max intervals | 4-8 repetitions; best for 200m-800m specialists    |
| 200-400m | Long VO2max intervals     | 3-5 repetitions; for 400m-1500m specialists        |


#### Rest Interval

- **Fixed rest**: 30-60 seconds (longer rest than threshold to allow partial recovery and maintain high intensity)
- **Percentage-based**: Rest = 30-50% of work time
- **Heart rate-based**: Wait until HR drops to approximately 120-130 bpm (approximately 65-70% HRmax) before next repetition

#### Intensity Targets


| Metric                     | Target                                                                                 |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Pace relative to CSS       | CSS pace - 3 to - 6 seconds per 100m (significantly faster than CSS)                   |
| Pace relative to race pace | 100m-400m race pace or slightly slower                                                 |
| Heart rate zone            | 92-98% HRmax (reaching HRmax by the third or fourth repetition)                        |
| RPE (6-20 Borg)            | 17-19                                                                                  |
| RPE (1-10 scale)           | 8-9                                                                                    |
| Lactate                    | 6.0-10.0 mmol/L post-set                                                               |
| Perceived effort           | "Very hard"; breathing heavily; can complete the set but each repetition is a struggle |


#### Volume Recommendations


| Swimmer Level | Per Set      | Per Session  | Notes                                                    |
| ------------- | ------------ | ------------ | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Beginner      | 200-400m     | 400-600m     | Introduce carefully; high risk of form breakdown         |
| Intermediate  | 500-1,000m   | 800-1,500m   | 1-2 sessions per week                                    |
| Advanced      | 1,000-2,000m | 1,500-3,000m | 2-3 sessions per week in appropriate training phase      |
| Elite         | 1,500-3,000m | 2,500-4,000m | Multiple high-intensity sessions per week in build phase |


#### Sample Sets

```
VO2max - Classic 100s
  8 x 100m on 1:20-1:35
  Pace: 100m race pace or 3-5s faster than CSS pace per 100m
  Rest: 20-35s built into send-off
  Focus: Strong finish on each 100; technique must hold under fatigue

VO2max - Descending 200s
  4 x 200m on 2:30-2:50
  Pace: At or slightly faster than 400m race pace
  Rest: 30-50s built into send-off
  Focus: Each 200 at same pace; do not let times drift

VO2max - Mixed Distance Pyramid
  1 x 400m on 5:00-5:30 (VO2max pace)
  1 x 200m on 2:30-2:50 (faster than 400 pace)
  1 x 100m on 1:15-1:25 (near 100m race pace)
  1 x 200m on 2:30-2:50
  1 x 400m on 5:00-5:30
  Rest: 60s between each
  Total: 1,300m at varying VO2max intensities
```

> **Coaching note:** VO2max sets should NOT be done on consecutive days. The neuromuscular and metabolic stress requires 48-72 hours for full recovery. Schedule VO2max work at the beginning or middle of the week, not before competition or time trials.

### 2.5 Sprint / Speed Development (Zone 6)

**Purpose:** Develop maximum speed, neuromuscular power, alactic energy system capacity, and race-specific speed. This is the highest intensity, shortest duration training.

#### The Science

Sprint training targets the phosphocreatine (ATP-PC) system:

- ATP-PC system provides energy for approximately 6-10 seconds of maximal effort
- Full phosphocreatine resynthesis requires 3-5 minutes of rest
- Sprint training improves neural drive, motor unit recruitment, and inter-muscular coordination
- Also develops the ability to tolerate and buffer high lactate levels when sprints are grouped

#### Interval Distance Selection


| Distance | Use Case                         | Notes                                         |
| -------- | -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| 10-15m   | Neuromuscular activation, starts | Underwater dolphin kick, wall pushes          |
| 15-25m   | Pure speed, acceleration         | Focus on maximum turnover from first stroke   |
| 25-50m   | Sprint speed with technique      | Most common sprint interval distance          |
| 50-75m   | Speed endurance                  | Transition from pure speed to speed endurance |


#### Rest Interval

- **For pure speed (neuromuscular focus)**: Rest = 1:12 to 1:20 work:rest ratio (3-5 minutes for a 15-25m sprint). This allows full phosphocreatine resynthesis so each rep is truly maximal.
- **For speed endurance (lactate tolerance)**: Rest = 1:3 to 1:6 work:rest ratio (60-180 seconds for a 25-50m sprint). Incomplete recovery forces the body to cope with accumulating lactate.

#### Intensity Targets


| Metric                    | Pure Speed                                  | Speed Endurance                               |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Effort                    | 95-100% maximum                             | 90-95% maximum                                |
| Pace                      | Faster than race pace                       | At or slightly slower than race pace          |
| RPE (1-10 scale)          | 10                                          | 8-9                                           |
| Lactate (pure speed)      | 2-4 mmol/L (short rest limits accumulation) | 8-14 mmol/L (grouped sprints with short rest) |
| Lactate (speed endurance) | N/A                                         | 8-14+ mmol/L                                  |


#### Volume Recommendations


| Swimmer Level | Per Set    | Per Session  | Notes                                                       |
| ------------- | ---------- | ------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Beginner      | 50-150m    | 100-200m     | Short distances, full recovery, focus on technique at speed |
| Intermediate  | 150-400m   | 300-600m     | Mix of pure speed and speed endurance                       |
| Advanced      | 400-800m   | 600-1,200m   | Significant sprint volume in competition phase              |
| Elite         | 600-1,200m | 1,000-2,000m | High-intensity sprint work in taper and competition phase   |


#### Sample Sets

```
Pure Speed - Neuromuscular
  8 x 25m from a dive
  Full effort, 100% maximum
  Rest: 3-4 minutes between each
  Focus: Explosion off wall, maximum stroke rate, perfect technique

Speed Endurance - Lactate Tolerance
  8 x 50m on 2:00-2:30 (pace: 50m race pace)
  Rest: Approximately 90-120s between reps
  Group as: 2 rounds of 4 with 3 min rest between rounds
  Focus: Hold race pace even as lactate accumulates; technique under pressure

Sprint Power - Short Rest
  16 x 25m sprint on 1:00-1:15
  Pace: Maximum sustainable sprint pace
  Rest: 35-50s between reps (incomplete recovery)
  Focus: Maintaining stroke quality as fatigue increases
```

> **Coaching note:** Sprint training should be done when swimmers are fresh, typically at the beginning of a session after the warm-up but before fatigue accumulates from other sets. Sprint technique degrades rapidly with fatigue, so quality is always prioritized over quantity.

### 2.6 Race-Pace Specificity

**Purpose:** Program the neuromuscular system to execute race pace with precision. This bridges the gap between fitness and performance, ensuring that accumulated fitness translates to competition success.

#### The Science

Race-pace training works through several mechanisms:

- **Neuromuscular patterning**: The nervous system learns to recruit the specific motor unit patterns needed at race pace
- **Pace awareness**: Swimmers develop an internal sense of race pace without looking at the clock
- **Energy system specificity**: Matches the aerobic/anaerobic energy contribution ratio of the target event
- **Psychological preparation**: Builds confidence in holding race pace under fatigue

#### USRPT (Ultra Short Race Pace Training)

One modern approach to race-pace specificity is USRPT, popularized by Dr. Brent Rushall. This method uses very short repetitions (15-50m for most events) at exact race pace with short rest intervals (20-45 seconds). The premise is that high-quality repetitions at race pace are more effective than longer intervals at slower paces.

Research on USRPT is limited but growing. Papadimitriou (2024) conducted a systematic review and concluded that "USRPT appears to be a submaximal variation of high-intensity interval training (HIIT) with low blood lactate relevance to swimming events" and that "the pace calculation must be implemented considering the different demands of each point of a swimming event."

#### Interval Design by Event


| Target Event | Interval Distance | Rest   | Repetitions | Pace                        |
| ------------ | ----------------- | ------ | ----------- | --------------------------- |
| 50m          | 10-25m            | 30-60s | 12-24       | Exact 50m race pace per 25m |
| 100m         | 15-50m            | 30-60s | 12-20       | Exact 100m race pace        |
| 200m         | 25-75m            | 30-45s | 12-20       | Exact 200m race pace        |
| 400m         | 50-100m           | 20-30s | 10-16       | Exact 400m race pace        |
| 800m         | 50-200m           | 15-25s | 8-16        | Exact 800m race pace        |
| 1500m        | 100-200m          | 10-20s | 6-12        | Exact 1500m race pace       |


> **Key distinction from VO2max:** Race-pace intervals target the exact pace of the competition, not maximum aerobic power. For a 200m swimmer, race pace may be significantly slower than VO2max pace. The interval distance is chosen to match the stroke count and breathing pattern of the race.

#### Sample Sets

```
Race Pace - 200m Freestyle Focus
  16 x 25m on :40-:45
  Pace: Exact 200m race pace per 25m (e.g., if target 200m is 2:00, each 25m in :15)
  Rest: 15-20s built into send-off
  Focus: Exact stroke count, exact breathing pattern, exact pace

Race Pace - 100m Freestyle Build
  4 rounds of:
    3 x 25m on :40 at 100m race pace
    1 x 25m on :40 at faster than race pace (build speed awareness)
  Rest: 60s between rounds
  Total: 16 x 25m

Race Pace - 400m IM Simulation
  8 x 50m on 1:00 (each stroke 2 x 50m)
  Pace: Exact 400m IM race pace per stroke
  Focus: Stroke transitions, turn execution at race pace
```

### 2.7 Recovery / Active Rest (Zone 0)

**Purpose:** Promote recovery between hard sessions and between repetitions within a set. Recovery swimming is not "nothing" -- it actively promotes lactate clearance, maintains mobility, and supports the parasympathetic nervous system.

#### The Science

Active recovery (light swimming) has been shown to enhance lactate clearance compared to passive recovery (complete rest). Shimoyama & Wada (2015) demonstrated that active recovery during rest periods in interval swimming leads to faster heart rate recovery and more efficient lactate removal. The mechanisms include:

- Continued blood flow through working muscles facilitates lactate transport to the liver for gluconeogenesis (Cori cycle)
- Light muscle activity maintains capillary perfusion
- Hydrostatic pressure from water immersion enhances venous return

#### Interval Characteristics


| Metric           | Target                                                       |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Pace             | Extremely slow; 20-30+ seconds per 100m slower than CSS pace |
| Heart rate       | < 60% HRmax; typically below 120 bpm                         |
| RPE (1-10 scale) | 1-2                                                          |
| Lactate          | Actively reducing elevated levels                            |


#### Recovery Within Sets (Between Repetitions)

The rest interval between hard repetitions is itself a training variable. Three approaches:

1. **Easy swim recovery**: Swim back at very slow pace. Best for aerobic and threshold sets.
2. **Pull buoy recovery**: Use pull buoy to rest legs while keeping upper body lightly active. Best for leg-focused sets or when preserving leg energy for the next hard repetition.
3. **Kick recovery**: Light flutter kick. Used when the set is upper-body focused and the coach wants legs to remain fresh.
4. **Wall rest**: Hold the wall. Used primarily in sprint sets where full phosphocreatine resynthesis is the goal.

#### Recovery Sessions (Dedicated Days)


| Component      | Recommendation                                                        |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Total distance | 1,500-3,000m (varies by level)                                        |
| Intensity      | Zone 1 only (conversational)                                          |
| Equipment      | Pull buoy, fins optional (fins add propulsion, reducing effort)       |
| Focus          | Technique drills, easy swimming, flexibility                          |
| Timing         | Day after hard session; or morning session after hard evening session |


#### Sample Recovery Set

```
Recovery Session - Post-Competition or Post-Hard Day
  Warm-up: 400m choice, very easy
  Drills: 8 x 50m drill on 1:00 (catch-up, fingertip drag, single-arm)
  Easy swim: 4 x 100m on 2:00 (pull buoy, easy pace)
  Cool-down: 200m easy
  Total: 1,400m
  Focus: Relaxation, stroke awareness, mental recovery
```

## 3. Interval Calculation Methods

### 3.1 CSS-Based Intervals

**Critical Swim Speed (CSS)** is the theoretical speed that a swimmer can sustain indefinitely without fatigue. It is analogous to critical power in cycling and critical velocity in running. The concept is based on the hyperbolic relationship between swimming speed and time to exhaustion (Wakayoshi et al., 1992; Ginn, 1998).

#### Calculating CSS

The standard method uses two time trials:

```
CSS = (D2 - D1) / (T2 - T1)

Where:
  D1 = 200m distance, T1 = time for 200m trial
  D2 = 400m distance, T2 = time for 400m trial

Example:
  200m time: 2:20 (140 seconds)
  400m time: 4:55 (295 seconds)
  CSS = (400 - 200) / (295 - 140) = 200/155 = 1.29 m/s
  CSS pace per 100m = 100/1.29 = 77.5 seconds = 1:17.5/100m
```

Alternative protocol (Scott et al., 2021): Three 200m and three 400m trials over three weeks, averaged for reliability. This protocol showed typical error of less than 0.04 m/s (CV < 4%) for backstroke, breaststroke, and front crawl. However, it is not practical for butterfly swimmers.

**3-minute all-out test** (Piatrikova et al., 2020): A more recent approach where swimmers complete a 3-minute all-out effort. The average speed over the final 30 seconds approximates CSS. This is more time-efficient than the 200/400 protocol but requires a maximal effort.

#### Deriving Training Paces from CSS


| Training Zone                    | Pace Relative to CSS | Example (CSS = 1:17/100m) |
| -------------------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Recovery (Zone 0)                | CSS + 20-30s/100m    | 1:37-1:47/100m            |
| Aerobic Base (Zone 1)            | CSS + 10-15s/100m    | 1:27-1:32/100m            |
| Aerobic Endurance (Zone 2)       | CSS + 5-10s/100m     | 1:22-1:27/100m            |
| Aerobic Threshold/Tempo (Zone 3) | CSS + 0-5s/100m      | 1:17-1:22/100m            |
| Lactate Threshold (Zone 4)       | CSS to CSS - 2s/100m | 1:15-1:17/100m            |
| VO2max (Zone 5)                  | CSS - 3-6s/100m      | 1:11-1:14/100m            |
| Sprint/Speed (Zone 6)            | 50m-100m race pace   | Event-dependent           |


> **Caveat:** CSS values degrade over a training season. Recalculate CSS every 4-6 weeks or at the end of each training phase. CSS typically improves by 1-3% over a 12-week training block in trained swimmers (Marinho et al., 2009).

#### Setting Send-Offs from CSS

```
Example swimmer: CSS = 1:17/100m

Aerobic set:
  10 x 100m at CSS + 5s/100m = 1:22/100m
  Target time per 100m: 1:22
  Rest needed: 15s
  Send-off: 1:37

Threshold set:
  8 x 100m at CSS pace = 1:17/100m
  Target time per 100m: 1:17
  Rest needed: 15s
  Send-off: 1:32

VO2max set:
  6 x 100m at CSS - 4s/100m = 1:13/100m
  Target time per 100m: 1:13
  Rest needed: 30s
  Send-off: 1:43
```

### 3.2 Heart Rate Based Intervals

#### HR Zones for Swimming

Heart rate monitoring in swimming has specific challenges but remains a useful tool when understood correctly.


| Zone                       | % HRmax | Description               | Swimming Application                   |
| -------------------------- | ------- | ------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Zone 0 (Recovery)          | < 60%   | Very light                | Active recovery, warm-up/cool-down     |
| Zone 1 (Aerobic Base)      | 60-75%  | Easy, conversational      | Base building, technique-focused swims |
| Zone 2 (Aerobic Endurance) | 75-82%  | Moderate, sustainable     | Extended aerobic sets                  |
| Zone 3 (Aerobic Threshold) | 82-88%  | Tempo, "comfortably hard" | Tempo intervals                        |
| Zone 4 (Lactate Threshold) | 88-92%  | Hard, threshold           | LT intervals                           |
| Zone 5 (VO2max)            | 92-98%  | Very hard                 | VO2max intervals                       |
| Zone 6 (Sprint)            | 98-100% | Maximum                   | Sprint sets, time trials               |


#### Calculating HRmax for Swimming

Swimming HRmax is typically 10-17 bpm lower than running HRmax for the same individual. Methods to determine swimming HRmax:

1. **Direct measurement**: 3 x 100m all-out with 30s rest; record highest HR on final 100m
2. **Age-predicted (adjusted)**: 208 - (0.7 x age) - 10 bpm (adjustment for water immersion)
3. **Field test**: Timed 400-800m all-out effort; highest HR sustained in final 100m

> **Important limitation:** The "cardiovascular drift" phenomenon in swimming means HR may not accurately reflect intensity in longer sets. As body temperature rises and dehydration occurs, HR increases even at constant pace. This is less pronounced in swimming than land sports due to water cooling, but still present in sessions over 60 minutes.

#### HR-Guided Rest Intervals

A practical method for individualized rest:

```
Aerobic set with HR-guided rest:
  8 x 100m
  Work: 100m at target pace (HR Zone 3, ~85% HRmax)
  Rest: Easy swim back until HR drops to Zone 2 (~78% HRmax)
  Next repetition starts when target HR is reached
```

This approach automatically adjusts rest for:

- Swimmer fitness level (fitter swimmers recover faster)
- Fatigue accumulation (rest increases as session progresses)
- Environmental conditions (warmer water = slower HR recovery)

#### Technology Notes

- **Chest strap monitors**: Most accurate for swimming (Polar H10, Garmin HRM-Pro). Optical wrist sensors are unreliable in water due to motion artifact.
- **Post-exercise HR**: Manual HR check at the wall immediately after finishing a rep gives a reasonable approximation but misses peak HR (which occurs 10-20 seconds after stopping).
- **Smart watches**: Garmin Swim 2, Apple Watch Ultra, and FORM goggles provide real-time HR but with variable accuracy during swimming.

### 3.3 Fixed Rest vs. Descending Rest

#### Fixed Rest

**Definition:** The same rest interval between all repetitions in a set.

**When to use:**

- Threshold and VO2max training (consistent metabolic stress)
- Race-pace training (consistent neuromuscular pattern)
- When the goal is pace consistency

**Advantages:**

- Simple to administer
- Predictable metabolic stress
- Easy to compare times across repetitions and sessions

**Example:**

```
8 x 100m on 1:30 (fixed 20s rest assuming 1:10 per 100m)
```

#### Descending Rest

**Definition:** Rest decreases as the set progresses.

**When to use:**

- Building lactate tolerance toward the end of a set
- Simulating race conditions (lactate accumulates toward the end)
- When the coach wants to progressively increase metabolic stress

**Advantages:**

- Progressive overload within a single set
- Simulates the increasing difficulty of a race
- Challenges mental toughness

**Example:**

```
10 x 100m
  Reps 1-3: on 1:40 (25s rest)
  Reps 4-6: on 1:35 (20s rest)
  Reps 7-10: on 1:30 (15s rest)
  Focus: Hold the same pace despite decreasing rest
```

#### Descending Send-Offs (Alternative Form)

Another approach is to keep the swimmer's target time constant while the send-off gets faster:

```
10 x 100m
  Goal: Swim each 100m in 1:15
  Send-offs: 1:40, 1:38, 1:36, 1:34, 1:32, 1:30, 1:28, 1:26, 1:24, 1:22
  Result: Rest decreases from 25s to 7s across the set
```

#### Which to Choose?


| Goal                     | Recommended Method |
| ------------------------ | ------------------ |
| Consistent pace training | Fixed rest         |
| Lactate tolerance        | Descending rest    |
| Race simulation          | Descending rest    |
| Technique under fatigue  | Descending rest    |
| Beginner swimmers        | Fixed rest         |
| Threshold development    | Fixed rest         |
| Speed endurance          | Descending rest    |

### 3.4 Send-Off Times

**Send-off time** is the standard coaching method for managing intervals. It is the time at which the next swimmer (or the same swimmer on the next repetition) leaves the wall, regardless of when the previous repetition was completed.

#### How Send-Offs Work

```
Send-off = Target time per repetition + Rest interval

Example:
  Target: 100m in 1:15
  Desired rest: 15 seconds
  Send-off: 1:30

  Swimmer leaves at :00, arrives at 1:15, waits 15 seconds, leaves at 1:30.
  If swimmer arrives at 1:18 (slower than target), rest is only 12 seconds.
  If swimmer arrives at 1:12 (faster than target), rest is 18 seconds.
```

#### The Coaching Power of Send-Offs

Send-offs automatically provide feedback:

- **Getting more rest than intended**: Swimmer is faster than target pace -- consider faster send-off
- **Getting less rest than intended**: Swimmer is slower than target pace -- consider slower send-off or adjust target
- **Consistent rest across repetitions**: Swimmer is hitting target pace -- interval is well-set

#### Send-Off Progression

```
Week 1:  10 x 100m on 1:40 (easy aerobic)
Week 2:  10 x 100m on 1:38 (slightly faster)
Week 3:  10 x 100m on 1:35 (progressing)
Week 4:  10 x 100m on 1:32 (testing)
Week 5:  Time trial / reassessment
```

#### Group Considerations

In a lane with swimmers of different abilities, the coach sets different send-offs:

```
Lane set: 10 x 100m
  Swimmer A (elite): on 1:15
  Swimmer B (advanced): on 1:25
  Swimmer C (intermediate): on 1:35
  Swimmer D (beginner): on 1:50
```

> **Practical tip:** Use a pace clock or a stopwatch with countdown functionality. Many pool clocks display the :00 and :30 (or :05 and :35) marks, making it easy for swimmers to know when to leave.

### 3.5 Percentage-Based Rest

**Definition:** Rest is calculated as a percentage of the work time.


| Training Zone              | Rest as % of Work Time | Example (100m in 1:20)      |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Recovery                   | 50-100%                | 40-80s rest                 |
| Aerobic Base (Zone 1)      | 15-25%                 | 12-20s rest                 |
| Aerobic Endurance (Zone 2) | 10-20%                 | 8-16s rest                  |
| Aerobic Threshold (Zone 3) | 10-15%                 | 8-12s rest                  |
| Lactate Threshold (Zone 4) | 5-15%                  | 4-12s rest                  |
| VO2max (Zone 5)            | 30-50%                 | 24-40s rest                 |
| Sprint - Pure Speed        | 400-800%               | 5-10 minutes for 15m sprint |
| Sprint - Speed Endurance   | 120-240%               | 60-120s for 25m sprint      |


#### Advantages

- Automatically scales with swimmer ability (faster swimmers get proportionally less rest in absolute time)
- Useful for mixed-ability groups
- Easy to calculate on the fly

#### Disadvantages

- Does not account for the non-linear relationship between intensity and recovery needs
- May give insufficient rest for VO2max work if calculated purely on percentage
- Less intuitive than fixed send-off times

### 3.6 Lactate-Guided Intervals

Lactate testing is the gold standard for setting training zones, though it requires specialized equipment (portable lactate analyzer) and trained personnel.

#### Lactate Threshold Determination Protocol

1. **Step test**: 4-6 x 200m or 400m at progressively increasing pace, with 20-30s rest between
2. **Blood sample**: Finger or earlobe prick after each repetition (at the wall, within 20 seconds)
3. **Analysis**: Plot lactate concentration vs. pace
4. **Determination**: The "turning point" where lactate rises sharply (often defined as 4.0 mmol/L, but individual thresholds vary from 2.5-6.0 mmol/L)

#### Training Zones by Lactate


| Zone                       | Lactate Range  | Description                         |
| -------------------------- | -------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Zone 0 (Recovery)          | < 1.5 mmol/L   | Complete recovery state             |
| Zone 1 (Aerobic Base)      | 1.5-2.0 mmol/L | Very light aerobic                  |
| Zone 2 (Aerobic Endurance) | 2.0-2.5 mmol/L | Moderate aerobic                    |
| Zone 3 (Aerobic Threshold) | 2.5-3.5 mmol/L | Aerobic threshold (AeT)             |
| Zone 4 (Lactate Threshold) | 3.5-5.0 mmol/L | Individual lactate threshold (OBLA) |
| Zone 5 (VO2max)            | 5.0-8.0 mmol/L | Supra-threshold, VO2max effort      |
| Zone 6 (Sprint)            | 8.0-14+ mmol/L | Maximum effort, high lactate        |


> **Important note:** The "4.0 mmol/L" threshold is a population average. Individual lactate threshold can vary dramatically -- elite swimmers may have thresholds at 5.0-6.0 mmol/L, while untrained individuals may exceed threshold at 2.5-3.0 mmol/L. Individual testing is essential for accurate zone setting.

#### Practical Lactate-Guided Set

```
Lactate-Guided Threshold Set
  10 x 100m at a pace that produces 3.5-4.5 mmol/L lactate
  Measure lactate after rep 3, rep 6, and rep 10
  If lactate is < 3.0: increase pace on remaining reps
  If lactate is > 5.0: decrease pace on remaining reps
  Target: Consistent lactate across the set
```

## 4. Periodization and Interval Selection

### 4.1 Macrocycle Phases

Swimming periodization follows a seasonal model, typically 6-12 months, divided into distinct phases. The distribution of training zones shifts dramatically across phases.

#### Phase 1: General Preparation (Base Building)

**Duration:** 8-16 weeks (longer for distance swimmers)


| Training Zone            | Percentage of Training | Key Characteristics                |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Zone 1-2 (Aerobic)       | 60-75%                 | High volume, low intensity         |
| Zone 3 (Tempo)           | 10-15%                 | Introduction of moderate intensity |
| Zone 4 (Threshold)       | 5-10%                  | Light threshold introduction       |
| Zone 5-6 (VO2max/Sprint) | 5-10%                  | Maintenance of speed               |
| Zone 0 (Recovery)        | 5-10%                  | Dedicated recovery sessions        |


**Interval characteristics:**

- Long repetitions (200-500m)
- Long rest relative to work (low metabolic stress)
- High total session volume
- Focus on technique consistency under moderate fatigue

**Sample week structure (intermediate swimmer, 25 km total):**

- Monday: 5,000m (80% Zone 1-2, 10% Zone 3, 10% Zone 0)
- Tuesday: 4,500m (70% Zone 1-2, 15% Zone 4, 15% Zone 0)
- Wednesday: 5,000m (60% Zone 1-2, 15% Zone 3, 15% Zone 5, 10% Zone 0)
- Thursday: 4,000m (70% Zone 1-2, 15% Zone 4, 15% Zone 0)
- Friday: 3,500m (80% Zone 1-2, 10% Zone 6, 10% Zone 0)
- Saturday: 4,000m (70% Zone 1-2, 15% Zone 3, 15% Zone 0)
- Sunday: Rest or light 2,000m recovery

#### Phase 2: Specific Preparation (Build Phase)

**Duration:** 6-12 weeks


| Training Zone      | Percentage of Training | Key Characteristics         |
| ------------------ | ---------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Zone 1-2 (Aerobic) | 40-50%                 | Maintained but reduced      |
| Zone 3 (Tempo)     | 15-20%                 | Increased tempo work        |
| Zone 4 (Threshold) | 15-25%                 | Primary training focus      |
| Zone 5 (VO2max)    | 10-15%                 | Increased VO2max work       |
| Zone 6 (Sprint)    | 5-10%                  | Speed maintenance           |
| Zone 0 (Recovery)  | 5-10%                  | Dedicated recovery sessions |


**Interval characteristics:**

- Moderate repetitions (100-300m)
- Moderate rest (increasing metabolic stress)
- Moderate-high total session volume
- Threshold work is the centerpiece

#### Phase 3: Pre-Competition (Sharpening)

**Duration:** 4-8 weeks


| Training Zone      | Percentage of Training | Key Characteristics            |
| ------------------ | ---------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Zone 1-2 (Aerobic) | 25-35%                 | Significantly reduced          |
| Zone 3 (Tempo)     | 15-20%                 | Maintained                     |
| Zone 4 (Threshold) | 15-20%                 | Maintained or slightly reduced |
| Zone 5 (VO2max)    | 15-20%                 | Increased                      |
| Zone 6 (Sprint)    | 10-15%                 | Significantly increased        |
| Zone 0 (Recovery)  | 5-10%                  | Recovery is critical           |


**Interval characteristics:**

- Short to moderate repetitions (25-200m)
- Race-pace specificity increases
- Total volume decreases
- Quality over quantity

#### Phase 4: Competition

**Duration:** Varies (meet season)


| Training Zone      | Percentage of Training | Key Characteristics         |
| ------------------ | ---------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Zone 1-2 (Aerobic) | 15-25%                 | Maintenance only            |
| Zone 3 (Tempo)     | 10-15%                 | Light maintenance           |
| Zone 4 (Threshold) | 10-15%                 | Light threshold maintenance |
| Zone 5 (VO2max)    | 15-20%                 | Maintained                  |
| Zone 6 (Sprint)    | 20-30%                 | Primary focus               |
| Zone 0 (Recovery)  | 10-15%                 | Active recovery critical    |


**Interval characteristics:**

- Very short repetitions (10-100m)
- Full or near-full recovery
- Race-pace precision
- Low total volume, very high quality

#### Phase 5: Taper

**Duration:** 10-21 days before major competition

Mujika (2010) established that optimal taper involves:

- **Volume reduction**: 41-60% reduction from pre-taper volume
- **Intensity maintenance**: Race-pace and faster work is maintained or slightly increased
- **Frequency maintenance**: Training frequency is not significantly reduced (to maintain neuromuscular patterns)


| Training Zone | Taper Week 1 | Taper Week 2 | Competition Week |
| ------------- | ------------ | ------------ | ---------------- |
| Zone 1-2      | 30-40%       | 20-30%       | 15-20%           |
| Zone 3        | 15-20%       | 10-15%       | 5-10%            |
| Zone 4        | 10-15%       | 5-10%        | 5%               |
| Zone 5        | 15-20%       | 15-20%       | 15-20%           |
| Zone 6        | 20-25%       | 25-30%       | 30-40%           |
| Zone 0        | 10-15%       | 15-20%       | 15-20%           |


**Interval characteristics during taper:**

- Very short, sharp repetitions
- Race-pace exactness
- Full recovery between reps
- Total session distance reduced by 40-60%
- Mujika (2010): "Training load should not be reduced at the expense of intensity during the taper"

### 4.2 Progressing Intervals Over a Training Cycle

The fundamental principle of interval progression is **progressive overload** -- systematically increasing the training stimulus over time.

#### Progression Methods


| Method                 | Description                                                | Example                                                                    |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Faster send-offs**   | Decrease the send-off time while keeping distance constant | Week 1: 10x100 on 1:40; Week 4: 10x100 on 1:30                             |
| **More repetitions**   | Increase the number of reps at the same send-off           | Week 1: 8x100; Week 4: 12x100                                              |
| **Longer repetitions** | Increase the distance of each rep at the same pace         | Week 1: 16x50; Week 4: 8x100 at same pace per 100m                         |
| **Shorter rest**       | Keep send-off same but swim faster (reduces rest)          | Week 1: 1:20 pace on 1:40 (20s rest); Week 4: 1:15 pace on 1:40 (25s rest) |
| **Harder conditions**  | Add equipment or change conditions                         | Week 1: 10x100 swim; Week 4: 10x100 with parachute                         |


#### Typical 4-Week Microcycle Progression

```
Week 1 (Introduction):
  10 x 100m on 1:35 (CSS + 5s/100m, 20s rest)

Week 2 (Build):
  10 x 100m on 1:33 (CSS + 3s/100m, 18s rest)

Week 3 (Peak):
  12 x 100m on 1:32 (CSS + 2s/100m, 17s rest, 2 extra reps)

Week 4 (Test/Recover):
  Time trial: 400m best effort
  OR lighter week: 8 x 100m on 1:35
```

### 4.3 Combining Interval Types in One Session

Most training sessions combine multiple zones. A typical structure:

```
Session Structure Template:
  1. Warm-up (500-1,000m): Zone 0-1
  2. Pre-set / Drill activation (400-800m): Zone 1-2, technique focus
  3. Main set (1,000-3,000m): Primary training zone
  4. Secondary set (400-1,000m): Secondary training zone
  5. Cool-down (200-400m): Zone 0

Example Session (Threshold + VO2max):
  Warm-up: 600m easy swim + 200m drill
  Pre-set: 8 x 50m build on 1:00 (build pace each 50m, Zone 2-3)
  Main set: 8 x 100m on 1:25 at threshold pace (Zone 4)
  Secondary: 6 x 50m on 1:00 at VO2max pace (Zone 5)
  Cool-down: 300m easy
  Total: ~3,300m
```

#### Principles for Combining Zones

1. **Hard before easy**: Place the most neurologically demanding work (sprint, VO2max) before metabolically demanding work (threshold). Exception: some coaches place threshold work first when the goal is to train threshold under fatigue.
2. **Threshold before VO2max**: If both are in the same session, threshold typically comes first (longer reps before shorter, faster reps).
3. **Sprint early in the session**: Speed work requires fresh neuromuscular system.
4. **Recovery between hard sets**: Allow 5-10 minutes easy swimming between main set and secondary set.
5. **Total session volume decreases as intensity increases**: A sprint-focused session may be 2,000-3,000m total; an aerobic session may be 5,000-8,000m.

### 4.4 Interval Training and Race Distance

The relationship between training intervals and target race distance follows these principles:


| Target Event | Primary Training Zones                                                 | Typical Interval Distances | Session Volume |
| ------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------- | -------------- |
| 50m          | Zone 6 (40%), Zone 5 (25%), Zone 4 (15%), Zone 3 (10%), Zone 0-1 (10%) | 10-75m                     | 2,000-4,000m   |
| 100m         | Zone 5 (30%), Zone 6 (25%), Zone 4 (20%), Zone 3 (15%), Zone 0-1 (10%) | 15-100m                    | 2,500-5,000m   |
| 200m         | Zone 4 (30%), Zone 5 (25%), Zone 3 (20%), Zone 6 (10%), Zone 0-1 (15%) | 50-200m                    | 3,500-6,000m   |
| 400m         | Zone 4 (35%), Zone 3 (25%), Zone 5 (20%), Zone 2 (10%), Zone 0-1 (10%) | 100-400m                   | 4,500-7,000m   |
| 800m         | Zone 3 (35%), Zone 4 (25%), Zone 2 (20%), Zone 5 (10%), Zone 0-1 (10%) | 200-800m                   | 5,500-9,000m   |
| 1500m        | Zone 2 (35%), Zone 3 (30%), Zone 4 (20%), Zone 1 (10%), Zone 0-1 (5%)  | 200-1500m                  | 6,000-10,000m  |


> **Note:** These percentages represent the distribution of training across a training macrocycle, not a single session. Even 50m sprinters need substantial aerobic base work, and even 1500m distance swimmers need some sprint work.

## 5. Event-Specific Considerations

### Sprinters (50-100m)

- **Higher kick contribution**: Sprinters derive 10-15% more propulsion from their kick than distance swimmers. Interval sets should include dedicated kick work.
- **Greater anaerobic contribution**: 50m freestyle is approximately 70-80% anaerobic; 100m is approximately 50-60% anaerobic (Hougland, 1978; Gastin, 2001).
- **Longer rest intervals**: Sprint work requires full recovery for neuromuscular quality.
- **Shorter total session distance**: 2,000-5,000m per session vs. 5,000-10,000m for distance swimmers.

### Mid-Distance (200-400m)

- **Balanced energy systems**: These events require both high aerobic capacity and significant anaerobic contribution.
- **Threshold training is central**: 200m and 400m performance is strongly correlated with lactate threshold pace.
- **Mixed interval distances**: Training should include both longer aerobic intervals and shorter sprint-quality intervals.

### Distance (800m-1500m+)

- **Aerobic dominance**: 800m+ events are 80-95% aerobic energy contribution.
- **Higher training volume**: Distance swimmers require significantly more total weekly distance.
- **Longer interval repetitions**: 200m, 400m, and even 800m repetitions are standard.
- **CSS is a strong predictor**: CSS correlates highly with 800m and 1500m performance (r > 0.90 in trained swimmers).
- **Gender differences**: Women typically have higher fat percentages and may have slightly different optimal training distributions, but the fundamental interval principles remain the same.

## 6. Swimmer Level Adjustments

### Beginner

- **Focus**: Technique, aerobic base, enjoyment
- **Intervals**: Short (25-50m), long rest, fixed send-offs
- **Intensity**: Predominantly Zone 1-2; minimal Zone 4+
- **Volume**: 1,500-3,000m per session
- **Key consideration**: Beginners should not train at lactate-producing intensities until they have a solid aerobic base (minimum 6 months of consistent training).

### Intermediate

- **Focus**: Building aerobic capacity, introducing threshold, refining technique at speed
- **Intervals**: Mixed (50-200m), moderate rest, fixed send-offs with occasional descending
- **Intensity**: Zone 1-4 regularly; Zone 5 occasionally; Zone 6 for short sets
- **Volume**: 3,000-5,000m per session
- **Key consideration**: This is where CSS testing becomes very useful for setting accurate training paces.

### Advanced

- **Focus**: Race-specific training, threshold and VO2max optimization, speed development
- **Intervals**: All distances (15-400m), rest varies by purpose, all send-off types
- **Intensity**: All zones; careful periodization of intensity
- **Volume**: 5,000-9,000m per session
- **Key consideration**: Lactate testing becomes increasingly valuable at this level for precise zone calibration.

### Elite

- **Focus**: Marginal gains, race-pace perfection, recovery optimization
- **Intervals**: Highly individualized, data-driven
- **Intensity**: All zones, precisely calibrated
- **Volume**: 7,000-12,000+ m per session
- **Key consideration:** At the elite level, training becomes highly individualized. What works for one elite swimmer may not work for another. Regular physiological testing (CSS, lactate curves, VO2max testing) is essential.
