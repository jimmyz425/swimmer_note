## Overview
Breathing in backstroke is fundamentally different from other strokes because the face is always above water. Natural, rhythmic breathing without breath-holding helps maintain relaxation and efficient stroke mechanics throughout the race.

**Difficulty:** Easy-Moderate

## Key Points to Remember
- **Breathe naturally** - Face is always above water
- **Regular rhythm** - Inhale during one arm recovery, exhale during other
- **Don't hold breath** - Continuous air exchange
- **Stay relaxed** - Breathing should feel easy
- **Timing matters** - Coordinate with stroke rhythm
- **Use nose, mouth, or both** - Your face is above water so both are available; many swimmers prefer mouth breathing to avoid water from splashes

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Holding breath** - Creates tension, wastes energy
- **Irregular breathing** - Breaks stroke rhythm
- **Over-breathing** - Taking too deep breaths
- **Breathing too fast** - Panting instead of steady rhythm
- **Forcing breath** - Creates neck tension

## Competitive Metrics

#### Drill 1: Rhythmic Breathing Set (4x200m, breathe on every 2nd stroke)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between breathing every 2nd vs every 4th stroke

> [!note] Targets
> - Competitive target: ≤3% time difference (breathing pattern doesn't disrupt rhythm). **Note:** Measuring ≤3% reliably requires electronic timing; hand-timed results have ±0.3s variability.
> - Self-check cue: In backstroke, breathing is naturally easier than freestyle since your face is above water. Focus on maintaining a steady rhythm without holding your breath.
> - **Important distinction:** Backstroke breathing is fundamentally different from freestyle. Since the face is always above water, there is no "underwater exhalation" concept. Exhale naturally through nose or mouth at any point in the stroke cycle.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Breathing rhythm: Regular, consistent pattern (visual assessment — no gasping or held breaths)
- No head movement: Head stays stable during breathing (no tilting/turning to seek air)
- Competitive target: Breathing does not disrupt stroke rhythm (stroke count and tempo remain consistent between different breathing patterns)

**Competitive Impact:** Disrupted breathing rhythm is a common cause of backstroke race fatigue, particularly among less experienced swimmers who hold their breath or breathe irregularly. **Note:** The claim that disrupted breathing is the "#1 cause" is a coaching assertion, not a research finding. Multiple factors contribute to race fatigue including pace management, turn efficiency, and overall fitness. Consistent, relaxed breathing is still important for maintaining race rhythm and reducing unnecessary tension.

#### Drill 2: Continuous Exhale Drill (10x25m, focus on steady breathing)
**Self-Check:** Perceived tension level — compare relaxed breathing vs. held-breath swimming for first 10 strokes of each lap

> [!note] Targets
> - Competitive target: No perceived tension difference between normal and focused breathing. **Note:** The "≤5% time difference" metric is not practical for this drill — focus on the *feeling* of relaxation instead.
> - Self-check cue: Breathe naturally and continuously. Unlike freestyle, there's no need for timed exhalation patterns — your face is always above water. Exhale steadily; inhale when it feels natural.
> - **Correction:** The original document mentioned "continuous exhale" and "exhale through nose the entire time face is above water." In backstroke, your face is always above water, so there is no "underwater exhalation" concept. Breathe naturally through nose and/or mouth.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- No body tension: Shoulders and core remain relaxed, no muscle clenching
- Consistent stroke length: No stroke length reduction when changing breathing patterns
- Competitive target: Breathing is steady and natural, no forced patterns or breath holding

**Competitive Impact:** Proper breathing technique helps maintain relaxation and efficient stroke mechanics. **Correction:** The claim that "proper breathing reduces lactic acid buildup by 15% during races" is **not supported by research**. While breath-holding does increase muscle tension and can accelerate fatigue, no peer-reviewed study has quantified a specific lactic acid reduction percentage from breathing technique in backstroke. Proper breathing does help maintain relaxation and oxygen delivery, but the 15% figure is fabricated.
