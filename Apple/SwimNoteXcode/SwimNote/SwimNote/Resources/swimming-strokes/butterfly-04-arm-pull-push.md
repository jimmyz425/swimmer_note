## Overview
The pull and push phase generates 70-80% of butterfly propulsion. Hands follow a keyhole or S-shaped pattern underwater, accelerating throughout. Power comes from the back muscles (lats), and hands should be fastest at the end of the push phase, finishing past the hips.
**Difficulty: Moderate**

## Key Points to Remember
- Follow keyhole or S-shaped pattern underwater (note: modern biomechanical research suggests the hand path may be more straight-back than the traditional keyhole; both patterns are taught and the optimal path may vary by swimmer)
- Accelerate hands throughout the pull
- Pull outward first, then inward, then outward again
- Push past hips with force
- Maintain high elbow position throughout
- Power comes from back muscles (lats), not just arms
- Hands should be fastest at the end of push phase

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Straight arm pull (note: some elite swimmers use a relatively straight arm pull with success, but for most swimmers a bent-arm catch is more efficient and reduces shoulder stress)
- Pulling too deep
- Slowing down at any point - acceleration is key
- Hands crossing under body midline
- Incomplete push phase (not finishing past hips)
- Pulling too wide

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Pull Buoy Dolphin** | Place pull buoy between legs, swim butterfly arms only. Focus on proper pull pattern. |
| **Single-Arm with Pull Buoy** | One arm extended, other arm stroking with pull buoy. Isolates arm mechanics. |
| **Paddle Swimming** | Use hand paddles to feel proper water catch and pull mechanics. Start small. |
| **Resistance Swimming** | Use resistance cord or parachute to build pull strength and emphasize acceleration. |

## Competitive Drills
#### Drill 1: Pull Only Drill (4x50m, pull buoy only, no kick)
**Self-Check:** Time per 50m pull, stroke count per lap

> [!tip] Tiered Pull Buoy 50m Targets
> | Level | Time per 50m (LCM) | Strokes per 25m | Notes |
> |-------|-------------------|-----------------|-------|
> | **Beginner** | 55-70s | 20-25 strokes | Developing pull strength; focus on high elbow |
> | **Intermediate** | 45-55s | 16-20 strokes | Consistent pull pattern; improving distance per stroke |
> | **Advanced** | 35-45s | 12-16 strokes | Strong lat engagement; efficient pull path |
> | **Elite** | 28-35s | 10-12 strokes | Maximal pull power; elite distance per stroke |

- Self-check cue: Feel your lats (back muscles) doing the work, not just your biceps/shoulders

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Pull path: Hands sweep out then in, forming a "keyhole" pattern under your body (note: modern research shows some elite swimmers use a straighter pull path)
- Push phase: Hands push all the way back to your thighs, accelerating through the finish
- Hand speed increases by ≥40% from catch to finish of pull (Advanced/Elite); ≥25% for Intermediate, ≥15% for Beginner

**Competitive Impact:** Pull phase generates approximately **70-80%** of butterfly propulsion during surface swimming, with the dolphin kick contributing the remaining 20-30%. The arms are the primary source of propulsion in butterfly.

#### Drill 2: Single-Arm Butterfly Drill (4x50m per arm, dolphin kick allowed)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between left and right single-arm swimming

> [!tip] Tiered Side-to-Side Balance Targets
> | Level | Time Difference | Notes |
> |-------|----------------|-------|
> | **Beginner** | ≤10% | Significant asymmetry expected; identify dominant side |
> | **Intermediate** | ≤7% | Improving symmetry; weaker side catching up |
> | **Advanced** | ≤5% | Good bilateral balance |
> | **Elite** | ≤3% | Near-perfect symmetry; both sides equally powerful |

- Self-check cue: Feel your hip undulate to drive the pull, not just your arm

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Full pull: Arm pulls all the way back to thigh before exiting
- No cross-over: Hand never crosses body center line during pull
- Pull power symmetry: Both left and right sides show similar hand speed and pull path

**Competitive Impact:** Side-to-side strength imbalance can cause asymmetrical stroke mechanics, which increases drag and reduces efficiency in butterfly. While the "3-4% race speed cost" is an estimate, even small asymmetries compound over race distance and are detectable through video analysis and split-time comparison.
