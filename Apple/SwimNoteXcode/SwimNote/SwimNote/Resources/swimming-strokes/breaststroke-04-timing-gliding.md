## Overview

Timing is the heart of breaststroke coordination. The essential sequence — PULL → BREATHE → KICK → GLIDE — must never be violated. The kick starts when the hands begin recovery forward (passing chest/shoulders is the trigger point), and the head should be down before the kick finishes. The glide phase should be held until momentum begins to slow, not for a fixed time count. Total stroke cycle is approximately 0.8-1.8 seconds, with glide phase percentage varying significantly by event distance.

**Difficulty:** Advanced

## Key Points to Remember

- **The essential sequence: PULL → BREATHE → KICK → GLIDE** - this order must never be violated
- **Kick starts when hands begin recovery forward** - passing chest/shoulders is the trigger point
- **Head should be down before kick finishes** - late head drop breaks the sequence
- **Hold glide until momentum begins to slow** - not a fixed time count, feel the speed decay
- **Glide phase percentage varies by event** - sprint (50-100m): 15-25% of cycle with shorter glide and higher tempo; distance (200m+): 25-40% of cycle with longer glide and more emphasis on efficiency
- **Total stroke cycle: approximately 0.8-1.8 seconds** - faster for sprint, slower for distance; pull+breathe takes 0.3-0.6s, kick takes 0.2-0.4s, glide takes 0.3-0.8s
- **Start kick when hands pass shoulders** - kicking during pull kills propulsion
- **Feel momentum before next stroke** - avoid starting next stroke too early

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Rushing through without glide** - eliminates the most efficient phase of breaststroke
- **Kicking during pull** - overlapping timing kills propulsion from both phases
- **Late kick timing** - head down after kick finishes breaks the sequence
- **No glide phase** - starting next stroke immediately wastes forward momentum
- **Starting next stroke too early** - must feel momentum drop before initiating next cycle

## Specific Drills

| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Two Kicks, One Pull** | For every arm pull, do two kicks. "Pull, kick, glide, kick, glide". 4x25m. |
| **Three-Second Glide Drill** | Hold streamline for full 3-count after each stroke. 4x25m. |
| **Count Strokes** | Count strokes per length. Fewer strokes = better efficiency. Elite: 10-18 per 25m (SCY/SCM), 12-20 per 25m (LCM). |
| **3-3-3 Drill** | 3 strokes breaststroke, 3 strokes freestyle, 3 strokes breaststroke. Repeat. |
| **Timing With Verbal Cues** | Say aloud: "Pull... Breathe... Kick... Glide". Synchronize stroke with words. |

## Competitive Metrics

#### Drill 1: Glide Hold Drill (4x25m, hold glide for 2 full seconds after each stroke)

**Self-Check:** Stroke count per 25m when holding 2s glide vs normal swimming

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Glide Efficiency — Stroke Count: 2s Glide vs Normal)
> - **Beginner:** 20-30% more strokes with 2s glide (glide significantly reduces efficiency)
> - **Intermediate:** 10-20% more strokes with 2s glide
> - **Advanced:** 5-10% more strokes with 2s glide
> - **Elite:** ≤5% stroke count difference (can hold long glide without losing efficiency)
>
> Self-check cue: Count "one-Mississippi, two-Mississippi" during each glide before starting next stroke
>
> **Note:** The goal is to minimize speed decay during glide. A well-streamlined swimmer loses less speed during the glide, so holding a 2s glide doesn't require many extra strokes to recover pace.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Glide position: Perfect streamline during entire glide phase, no arm/leg movement
- Glide duration: Exactly 2s per glide (consistent across all strokes)
- Competitive target: Minimize speed decay during glide. Speed naturally decreases during the glide phase as propulsion ceases; the goal is to maintain a streamlined position so that speed drops as slowly as possible. *(Note: "20% speed loss" is a coaching heuristic; actual speed decay depends on the swimmer's initial velocity and drag profile.)*

**Competitive Impact:** Proper glide technique contributes significantly to breaststroke efficiency by reducing drag between propulsion phases. The commonly cited "20-30% of total speed" refers to the relative importance of maintaining glide efficiency — poor glide position wastes the forward momentum generated by the kick and pull. *(Note: this is a coaching heuristic, not a direct speed measurement.)*

#### Drill 2: Tempo Training (4x200m set, use waterproof metronome set to your race tempo)

**Self-Check:** Percentage of strokes that sync exactly with the metronome beep

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Tempo Synchronization)
> - **Beginner:** ≥70% of strokes synced with target tempo
> - **Intermediate:** ≥80% of strokes synced
> - **Advanced:** ≥90% of strokes synced
> - **Elite:** ≥95% of strokes synced with consistent rhythm
>
> Self-check cue: Sync the start of your pull exactly with the metronome beep
>
> **Note:** Consistent tempo is critical in breaststroke. Research on world-class breaststrokers shows stroke rates of 55-65 cycles/min for 50m, 45-55 for 100m, and 35-45 for 200m. Large tempo fluctuations within a race waste energy and disrupt rhythm.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Stroke cycle timing: Perfect PULL → BREATHE → KICK → GLIDE sequence, no overlaps
- Glide ratio: Glide phase percentage varies by event — **15-25% for sprint** (50-100m), **25-40% for distance** (200m+). Sprint breaststroke uses shorter glides and higher tempo; distance uses longer glides for efficiency. *(Corrected: document previously stated "40-50% optimal for sprint" which is inaccurate; sprint breaststroke actually uses shorter, not longer, glides.)*
- Competitive target: No timing variation between breathing and non-breathing strokes

**Competitive Impact:** Consistent tempo is critical in breaststroke. Research on world-class breaststrokers shows stroke rates of **55-65 cycles/min for 50m**, **45-55 for 100m**, and **35-45 for 200m**. Large tempo fluctuations within a race waste energy and disrupt rhythm. *(Note: the "0.1s = 1.5s per 100m" formula is a coaching approximation; actual time cost depends on the swimmer's baseline speed and how the tempo change affects technique.)*
