## Overview
The dolphin kick originates from the hips and core using a whipping motion with both legs simultaneously. Two kicks occur per stroke cycle -- a smaller first kick and a larger, more powerful second kick. Flexible ankles and hip-driven undulation are essential for maximum propulsion.
**Difficulty: Easy to Moderate**

## Key Points to Remember
- Kick originates from **HIPS and CORE**, not knees
- Use a whipping motion with both legs simultaneously
- **Two kicks per stroke cycle**: smaller first kick, larger second kick
- Maintain flexible ankles for maximum propulsion
- Keep legs relatively straight during upward motion
- Power comes from core muscles and glutes

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Bending knees excessively (bicycle kick)
- Kicking too deep - creates drag
- Weak second kick (should be more powerful)
- Stiff ankles limiting propulsion
- Kicking from thighs instead of whole body undulation

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Vertical Dolphin Kick** | In deep water, hold arms out of water or crossed at chest. Kick to keep head above water. |
| **Kick on Back with Arms at Sides** | Lie on back, arms at sides, dolphin kick. Look for water boiling at feet. |
| **Kick on Side** | Lie on side with one arm extended, practice dolphin kick. Develops body roll and symmetry. |
| **Kick with Fins** | Use short training fins to feel proper whip motion and develop ankle flexibility. |
| **Underwater Dolphin Kick** | Push off wall in streamline, perform rapid dolphin kicks underwater. Builds power and timing. |

## Competitive Metrics
#### Drill 1: Vertical Dolphin Kick (deep water, arms extended overhead in streamline)
**Self-Check:** Hold vertical position with chin above water, no arm movement

> [!tip] Tiered Vertical Dolphin Kick Hold Times
> | Level | Streamline Arms Overhead | Fists Closed | Crossed at Chest (easier) |
> |-------|-------------------------|--------------|---------------------------|
> | **Beginner** | 5-10s | 3-5s | 15-20s |
> | **Intermediate** | 10-20s | 5-10s | 20-30s |
> | **Advanced** | 20-30s | 10-15s | 30-45s |
> | **Elite** | 45-60s | 15-20s | 60-90s |
>
> *Note: Streamline arm position overhead is significantly harder than crossed-chest variation. Age-group swimmers typically achieve 5-15 seconds. Fist variation removes ~20% propulsive surface area.*

- Self-check cue: If your chin drops below water, your kick is inefficient (you're not generating enough upward propulsion)

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Kick amplitude: ≤40cm from highest to lowest point of feet (Beginner: up to 50cm acceptable as technique develops)
- Kick tempo: For surface butterfly with 2 kicks per stroke cycle, kick frequency equals 2x stroke rate (~70-90 kicks/min at 35-45 cycles/min). For underwater dolphin kick (UDK) sets, elite swimmers can achieve 2.5-3.5 kicks/second (~150-210 kicks/min). Note: BPM is not a standard swimming metric; stroke cycles per minute is the more common measure.
- Ankles plantar-flexed (pointed) during entire kick cycle

**Competitive Impact:** Biomechanical research shows the dolphin kick contributes approximately **20-30%** of total butterfly propulsion, with arms providing the remaining 70-80%. The second kick (timed with arm push) contributes more than the first. Underwater dolphin kick is approximately **10-20% faster** than surface swimming due to elimination of wave drag (Gourgoulis et al., Marinho et al.). The "30% faster" and "50-60% propulsion" claims found in older coaching materials have been revised downward based on peer-reviewed findings.

#### Drill 2: Underwater Dolphin Kick Set (10x25m, off walls, no surface stroke)
**Self-Check:** Distance traveled off each wall with dolphin kicks before breaking surface

> [!tip] Tiered UDK Distance Targets
> | Level | Off Start Wall | Off Turn Wall | Kicks to 15m |
> |-------|---------------|---------------|--------------|
> | **Beginner** | 5-8m | 5-7m | 10-14 (large amplitude) |
> | **Intermediate** | 8-12m | 8-10m | 7-10 |
> | **Advanced** | 12-15m | 10-13m | 5-8 |
> | **Elite** | 15m (FINA limit) | 13-15m | 4-6 (compact, fast) |
>
> *Note: FINA/World Aquatics rules limit underwater swimming to 15m after start and each turn. Kick count varies by swimmer -- depends on kick amplitude and body size. Elite swimmers use fewer, more powerful kicks.*

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Undulation wave: Travels from head to toe in one continuous motion
- Down kicks produce approximately 1.5x more power than up kicks
- Body remains in tight streamline for entire underwater phase

**Competitive Impact:** Underwater dolphin kicks are the fastest form of human locomotion in water for distances under 25m. Elite swimmers maximize distance off each wall (up to the 15m FINA limit). The exact contribution to total race speed varies by event distance and swimmer; the ~20% figure is a commonly cited estimate at elite levels but should be treated as a guideline rather than a precise measurement.
