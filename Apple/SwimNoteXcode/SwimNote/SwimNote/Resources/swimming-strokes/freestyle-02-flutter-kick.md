## Overview
The flutter kick provides propulsion and helps maintain body position. It originates from the hips with a compact, fast motion. Mastering the flutter kick is essential for both sprint and distance swimming.
**Difficulty: Easy-Moderate**

## Key Points to Remember
- **Kick originates from the hips** - Not from knees
- **Legs relatively straight** - Only slight knee bend (15-30 degrees)
- **Compact amplitude** - Kick size about 12-16 inches (30-40cm) maximum; **Note:** This is a coaching guideline. Amplitude varies with body size and flexibility. The key is keeping the kick within the body's "shadow" (no feet breaking the surface excessively during horizontal swimming).
- **Relaxed, flexible ankles** - Ankles should be loose, feet pointed naturally
- **Fast tempo** - Quick, continuous kicks (not slow and powerful)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Bicycle kicking** - Excessive knee bend, kicks from knees instead of hips
- **Kick too wide/large** - Creates excessive drag, slows you down
- **Stiff ankles** - Reduces propulsion efficiency
- **Kicking too slow** - Loses momentum and propulsion
- **Not pointing toes** - Creates drag instead of propulsion

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Kick with Board** | Hold kickboard extended, kick focusing on hip-driven motion. Isolate legs to perfect technique. |
| **Vertical Kicking** | Kick vertically in deep water, arms crossed at chest. Forces proper hip-driven kick (knee-bending sinks you). |
| **Kick on Side (6-1-6)** | Kick 6 times on right side, take 1 stroke, kick 6 times on left side. Develops kick timing with rotation. *Also useful for [[swimming-strokes/freestyle-03-breathing-technique]] and [[swimming-strokes/freestyle-04-body-rotation]] practice.* |
| **Fin Training** | Use swim fins to develop ankle flexibility and feel proper kick amplitude. |
| **Streamline Kick** | Kick in streamline position (no board) to develop whole-body coordination. |

## Competitive Drills
#### Drill 1: Vertical Kicking (deep water, no board)
**Self-Check:** Hold vertical position with arms crossed at chest, chin above water

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** 15-30 seconds continuous hold
> - **Intermediate:** 30-60 seconds
> - **Advanced:** 60-90 seconds
> - **Elite:** ≥90 seconds, ≥30 seconds with fists closed
>
> Self-check cue: If you sink below mouth level, your kick is inefficient
>
> **Note:** These hold times are widely used as coaching benchmarks (particularly in US college and club programs) but are not validated by formal research as predictive of race performance. They are useful as a general indicator of kick strength and endurance. The fists-closed elite benchmark (≥30s) is an advanced variation that further tests ankle flexibility and kick efficiency.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Knee bend: ≤30 degrees at maximum bend (estimate visually — slight bend only, no bicycle motion). **Note:** Some knee bend is necessary and natural; the key is that the bend is driven by hip extension, not active knee flexion. Excessive bend (>45 degrees) indicates "bicycle kicking."
- Kick tempo: ≥120 bpm for sprint/6-beat kick patterns (fast, continuous flutter — not slow and powerful). **Note:** This tempo target applies to 6-beat kick swimmers. Distance swimmers using 2-beat or 4-beat kicks will have lower kick rates (~60-80 bpm for 2-beat). Tempo is event- and kick-pattern-dependent.
- Competitive target: Ankles pointed to ≤10 degrees from leg line (flexible ankles = significantly more propulsion). **Note:** Plantar flexion range of motion is a measurable predictor of kick efficiency. Elite swimmers typically have 50+ degrees of plantar flexion. Stiff ankles (limited plantar flexion) create drag by presenting the sole of the foot as a braking surface during the up-kick phase.

**Competitive Impact:** Efficient kick provides approximately 10-15% of race propulsion in distance events and up to 25-30% in sprint events. **Research basis:** Zamparo et al. (1996) used the active drag method to isolate kick contribution, finding kick contribution ranges from ~10% in distance swimming to ~30%+ in sprint swimming, with significant individual variation. Research suggests women may derive a slightly higher percentage of propulsion from their kick due to generally greater ankle flexibility and higher natural kick rates, though the magnitude of this gender difference varies across studies. **Note:** These percentages are event- and individual-dependent. Some elite distance swimmers (e.g., Sun Yang) use powerful 4-beat or 6-beat kicks even in longer events, while some elite sprinters rely heavily on pull. Kick also stabilizes body rotation. Strong vertical kick translates to better horizontal kick mechanics.

#### Drill 2: Streamline Kick (no board, 25m set)
**Self-Check:** Time per 25m

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Streamline Kick — No Board)
> - **Beginner:** ≤30 seconds per 25m
> - **Intermediate:** ≤25 seconds per 25m
> - **Advanced:** ≤22 seconds per 25m
> - **Elite:** ≤18 seconds per 25m (male), ≤20 seconds per 25m (female)
>
> Self-check cue: Count kicks on one leg to track tempo consistency. Focus on hip-driven motion, not knee-driven.
>
> **Note:** These are coaching benchmarks. The gender differentiation at the elite level reflects typical physiological differences in sprint speed, though individual variation is significant. For reference, elite 50m freestyle world record holders complete 25m in ~10-11s with full stroke and kick, so ≤18s for kick-only is a demanding target.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Kick amplitude: ≤40cm (16 inches) from highest to lowest point — compact, not wide. **Note:** Amplitude varies by body size and leg length. The key is keeping the kick within the body's "shadow" — feet should not break the water surface significantly during horizontal swimming.
- No bicycle kick: Knees do not bend more than 30 degrees
- Competitive target: Kick originates from hips (no visible upper leg lag — thighs should initiate motion)

**Competitive Impact:** Every improvement in kick efficiency contributes to overall speed and body position. Kick quality is especially critical in sprint events where propulsion demands are highest.
