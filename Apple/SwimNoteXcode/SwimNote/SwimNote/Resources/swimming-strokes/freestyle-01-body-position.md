## Overview
Body position and streamline form the foundation for all other freestyle techniques. A proper body position minimizes drag and allows all subsequent techniques to work efficiently. This is considered the easiest technique to learn but is critical to master first.
**Difficulty: Easiest (Foundation for all other techniques)**

## Key Points to Remember
- **Look down, not forward** - Head position should be neutral, eyes looking at the pool bottom
- **Keep hips high** - Hips should be near the surface; dropped hips create massive drag
- **Maintain a slight body tilt** - Body should be angled slightly forward (head lower than feet)
- **Keep legs near surface** - Legs should not sink deep; they add to streamline

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Looking forward instead of down (lifts head, drops hips)
- Letting hips and legs sink ("plowing" through water)
- Over-arching the back
- Head too high or too deep

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Torpedo/Kick on Back** | Float on back with arms streamlined above head, kick gently. Focus on keeping hips at surface. |
| **Kick on Side** | Kick on your side with one arm extended forward, other arm resting on hip. 6 kicks per side, then switch. Develops body position awareness. |
| **Streamline Push-offs** | Practice tight streamline off every wall push-off. Arms pressed together, head between biceps. |
| **Balance Drill** | Float face-down, arms at sides. Press chest down gently to raise hips. Feel the "T" position balance. |

## Competitive Metrics
#### Drill 1: Streamline Push-offs (measure distance, not time)
**Self-Check:** Push off in tight streamline and glide (no kicking). Mark where your speed drops so low you must initiate a stroke or kick. Measure this distance from the wall.

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Glide Distance Before Speed Decay)
> - **Beginner:** 3-5m before speed drops below useful racing pace
> - **Intermediate:** 5-7m
> - **Advanced:** 7-10m
> - **Elite:** 10-12m+ (world-class swimmers with exceptional streamlines)
>
> Why measure distance, not time? A push-off starts at ~2.0-2.5 m/s but decelerates rapidly due to quadratic drag. By 5m, speed typically drops to ~1.5-1.7 m/s even with a tight streamline. Time-to-distance depends on your initial push-off force, making it an unreliable metric for glide *quality*. Distance shows how well your form minimizes drag.
>
> **Important:** These distances are for *pure glide* (no dolphin kick). With underwater dolphin kicking, elite swimmers can travel 10-15m while maintaining racing pace. The FINA 15m rule limits underwater swimming to 15m after starts and turns. Push-off speed values (~2.0-2.5 m/s) are consistent with biomechanics literature (Lyttle et al., 1998; 1999).
>
> Self-check cue: Count "one-Mississippi..." to estimate glide *duration* — elites typically maintain useful speed for ~3-5 seconds of glide before kicking must begin.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Hip height: Hips at or above shoulder line in side-angle footage (relative measure, more practical than absolute depth)
- Streamline alignment: Head fully between biceps, arms pressed straight together, no visible body arch
- Competitive target: 100% of push-offs maintain tight streamline through the full glide phase (no arms drifting, no body arch forming)

**Competitive Impact:** Better push-off technique saves ~0.05-0.1s per wall. In short course (25m pool): 4 push-offs per 100m (start + 3 turns), saving ~0.2-0.4s. In long course (50m pool): 2 push-offs per 100m (start + 1 turn), saving ~0.1-0.2s. *The larger benefit is maintaining momentum into the first strokes, not just the push-off itself.* **Note:** These time savings are coaching estimates based on turn performance studies (Mason et al., 2013). Actual savings vary by swimmer, wall type, and how time is measured (wall-to-wall vs. full race). The contribution of a better streamline off the wall to overall race time is small but accumulates across multiple turns.

#### Drill 2: Side Kick (no board, 25m set)
**Self-Check:** Time per 25m

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≤35 seconds per 25m
> - **Intermediate:** ≤30 seconds per 25m
> - **Advanced:** ≤25 seconds per 25m
> - **Elite:** ≤22 seconds per 25m
>
> Self-check cue: Kick only hard enough to keep hips at surface; focus on stability over speed
>
> **Note:** These times are coaching benchmarks, not research-validated standards. Actual times depend heavily on body composition, buoyancy, and pool conditions. The key metric is *consistency* between left and right sides rather than absolute speed.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Body tilt angle: ≤10 degrees from horizontal (estimate visually — body should appear nearly flat). **Note:** During side kick, the body is intentionally tilted (on its side), so this refers to the deviation from the ideal side-kick position — the body should not roll toward the surface or sink toward the bottom.
- No hip sag: Hips stay near surface for entire 25m (visible drop indicates over-kicking or poor core engagement)
- Competitive target: <2 seconds time difference between left and right side kicking (use stopwatch for each side). **Note:** This is a practical benchmark for assessing kick symmetry. A larger time difference suggests one side has better body position, kick efficiency, or stability than the other.

**Competitive Impact:** Consistent side position significantly reduces drag compared to flat swimming with dropped hips. Better side-kick stability translates directly to better balance during full-stroke swimming. *Quantifying exact drag reduction is complex and varies by body type and technique.*
