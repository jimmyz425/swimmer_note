## Overview
The pull phase is where the majority of propulsion happens in freestyle. After setting the catch, the hand and forearm pull straight back with the body moving past the anchored hand. The pull should accelerate from catch to finish, maintaining a high elbow position throughout, and exit cleanly at hip level.
**Difficulty: Hard (Where propulsion happens)**

## Key Points to Remember
- **Pull straight back** - Hand/forearm path is relatively straight, not S-shaped
- **Body moves past hand** - Hand anchors, body travels past it
- **Maintain high elbow** - Elbow stays above wrist throughout pull
- **Accelerate through pull** - Speed increases from catch to finish
- **Pull in line with body** - Don't pull under body center or wide
- **Finish at hip** - Exit hand at hip level, not past hip

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **S-pull pattern** - Hand sweeps out-in-out (old technique, inefficient)
- **Dropped elbow pull** - Elbow collapses during pull
- **Pulling too wide** - Hand sweeps wide outside shoulder line
- **Pulling under body** - Hand crosses under centerline
- **Over-finishing** - Hand pushes past hip, causes delay
- **Not accelerating** - Pull speed constant instead of increasing

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Doggy Paddle** | High elbow, pull straight back, feel the propulsion. Excellent pull mechanics drill. |
| **Single-Arm Pull Focus** | Swim with one arm only, focus on perfect pull pathway. |
| **Fist Drill Pull** | Fist swimming, focus on forearm pressure during pull. |
| **Catch-Up Pull** | Catch-up emphasizing pull path and finish point. |
| **Ankle Band Swimming** | No kick allowed, forces efficient pull for propulsion. |

## Competitive Metrics
#### Drill 1: Single-Arm Pull (4x50m per arm, pull buoy only, no kick)
**Self-Check:** Time per 50m single-arm swimming

> [!note] Targets
> - Competitive target: ≤2x your normal 50m freestyle time (shows efficient pull mechanics). Expect this to be challenging — single-arm swimming removes half your propulsion and eliminates the stabilizing effect of the opposite arm. **Note:** Actual time ratios vary; some swimmers achieve 1.5-1.8x, others may take 2.5-3x due to the added challenge of maintaining body position and direction with one arm. Use this as a personal baseline rather than an absolute standard.
> - Self-check cue: Feel your lats (back muscles) doing the work, not your biceps

**Video Check (underwater footage, if available):**
- Pull path: Hand travels straight back parallel to body line (no S-curve pattern, no cross-over under body)
- Exit point: Hand exits at or near hip level (not early, not late)
- Competitive target: Pull accelerates from catch to finish (speed increases through the pull — visible as hand moving faster at exit than at entry)

> [!tip] Measuring Pull Speed
> Claims of "hand speed increases by ≥30%" require motion-tracking software. For practical self-coaching, focus on the *feeling* of acceleration: does the pull feel like it builds power, or does it feel constant/slow throughout?

**Competitive Impact:** Modern 3D motion analysis research (Schleihauf, 1979; Sanders, 1999-2007) supports a relatively straight pull path over the exaggerated S-curve popularized by Counsilman (1971). However, elite swimmers do not pull perfectly straight — they use subtle inward sweep during the insweep phase and slight outward movement during the upsweep for hand stability and optimal force vector alignment. The key finding is that the dramatic S-shape is inefficient; a relatively straight path with minor lateral adjustments is optimal. **Note:** Schleihauf's original 3D analysis showed that elite swimmers' hand paths have modest lateral deviations (5-15cm from a straight line), not the large S-curves previously taught. *Exact efficiency gains depend on the swimmer's baseline technique.*

#### Drill 2: Pull Buoy Only Swimming (4x100m, no kick allowed)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between pull buoy swimming vs normal swimming

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≤15% time increase
> - **Intermediate:** ≤10% time increase
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≤5% time increase (pull is primary propulsion)
>
> Self-check cue: Maintain same stroke tempo as normal swimming. Don't slow your stroke rate to compensate for lost kick.
>
> **Note:** These percentages represent *time increase* with a pull buoy (which eliminates kick but adds buoyancy). The pull buoy reduces leg drag by providing flotation, so the time increase from eliminating kick is less than the kick's propulsion contribution would suggest. The ≤5% target for advanced/elite swimmers reflects the fact that their pull is already the dominant propulsion source. These are coaching benchmarks; individual results vary.

**Video Check (underwater footage, if available):**
- High elbow maintained: Elbow stays above wrist throughout entire pull phase
- No arm crossing: Hand never crosses body center line during pull
- Competitive target: Pull power same on both left and right sides (assess by comparing time per 50m on each side)

**Competitive Impact:** Pull efficiency is the single largest determinant of swimming speed. Pull buoy swimming isolates the pull and reveals how much propulsion comes from your arms and core vs. your kick.
