# Breaststroke Swimming Technique: Complete Training Reference
## Ranked by Difficulty: Easiest to Hardest

> Part of [[areas/swimming-strokes|Swimming Strokes Research]] · Code project: [[projects/swimmer-notes-app|Swimmer's Notes App]]

> [!important] Note on Competitive Targets and Metrics
> All "Competitive target" values in this document are **guidelines**, not absolutes. Actual performance depends on:
> - **Swimmer level** (age-group, collegiate, elite) — tiered targets are provided where possible
> - **Body type and physiology** — longer limbs, different buoyancy affect metrics
> - **Pool conditions** — water temperature, depth, and current affect times
> - **Measurement method** — hand-timed results have ±0.3s variability; phone video cannot measure sub-centimeter or sub-100ms precision
>
> **Research methodology for this document (reviewed 2026-04):**
> - Quantitative claims were cross-referenced against published biomechanics research, World Aquatics rules, and established coaching literature (Maglischo "Swimming Fastest", Counsilman "The Science of Swimming", peer-reviewed studies in Sports Biomechanics and Journal of Sports Sciences).
> - Some specific numbers (drag percentages, exact time-cost formulas, precise angle measurements) are **coaching approximations** rather than rigorously measured scientific values. These are flagged throughout the document.
> - The claim that breaststroke kick generates "60-70% of propulsion" is commonly cited in coaching literature but represents the **upper range**; recent biomechanical studies using 3D motion analysis suggest kick contribution is closer to **50-60%** for elite sprinters and lower for distance swimmers. Both ranges are noted where relevant.
> - Video-based measurements (hip height in cm, body angles in degrees) require **high-speed underwater cameras** with calibration references. Phone video provides only rough approximations. Focus on **directional improvement** rather than hitting exact numbers.
> - Glide phase percentage varies significantly by event: **sprint** breaststroke has shorter glides (15-25% of cycle), **distance** breaststroke has longer glides (25-40% of cycle). Single values should be understood as event-dependent.

## Overview

Breaststroke is widely considered the most technically demanding swimming stroke. Unlike other strokes where arms and legs work simultaneously, breaststroke requires precise coordination with a specific timing sequence: **Pull → Breathe → Kick → Glide**. Poor technique can actually make you slower or even move you backward.

**Estimated Difficulty Distribution:**
- Body Position/Glide: ~20% of learning challenge
- Kick Technique: ~40% - Most Challenging Component
- Arm Pull: ~15%
- Timing/Coordination: ~25%

> [!note] On Difficulty Rankings
> These rankings are general guidelines. Actual difficulty varies by swimmer:
> - **Kick Mechanics** (Level 5): Widely considered the most challenging component (~40% of learning challenge). The whip kick motion is counterintuitive and requires significant ankle flexibility training.
> - **Timing & Gliding** (Level 4): Many swimmers find timing harder than individual components. Each piece may work in isolation but combining them is the real challenge.
> - **Body Position** (Level 1): Arguably the most important foundation -- poor body position makes every other technique harder.

## Key Mental Cues

- **"Heels to bum"** - Correct knee bend
- **"Narrow knees"** - Reduce drag
- **"Fast hands"** - Accelerate through pull and recovery
- **"Snap the feet"** - Power phase of kick
- **"Long and strong"** - Extend fully during glide
- **"Pull... Breathe... Kick... Glide"** - Timing sequence

### Image References

**Key Positions to Visualize:**

1. **Whip Kick Foot Position (Critical)**
   - Feet turned OUTWARD before propulsion
   - Heels close to buttocks
   - Knees narrow (not wide)
   - Think: "Feet like duck feet" - turned out, ready to whip

2. **Narrow Knee Position**
   - Knees hip-width or closer
   - NOT spread wide
   - Heels come to bum, not knees to chest
   - Think: "Keep knees together, feet apart"

3. **Arm Pull Phases**
   - Outsweep: narrow, just past shoulders
   - Insweep: hands to chest, elbows HIGH
   - Shoot: arms drive forward quickly
   - Think: "Small outsweep, big insweep, fast shoot"

4. **Streamline Glide Position**
   - Arms extended forward together
   - Head looking down
   - Body flat at surface
   - Think: "Arrow gliding through water"

5. **Timing Sequence Visual**
   ```
   PULL → BREATHE → KICK → GLIDE

   Pull + Breathe: ~0.3-0.6s (arms sweep, breath during insweep)
   Kick: ~0.2-0.4s (heels to bum, feet out, snap)
   Glide: ~0.3-0.8s (hold streamline, varies by event)

   Note: Exact phase durations vary by event distance and swimmer.
   Sprint breaststroke has shorter glides; distance has longer glides.
   ```
