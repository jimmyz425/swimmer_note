

# Butterfly — Body Position & Horizontal Alignment

## Overview
Maintaining a flat, horizontal body position on the water surface is the foundation of efficient butterfly swimming. The chest press initiates the undulation motion, and proper head position keeps the hips high. This is the easiest technique to learn but critical for all subsequent skills.
**Difficulty: Easiest**

## Key Points to Remember
- Maintain a flat, horizontal body position on the water surface
- Keep head neutral, looking down at the pool bottom during glide phases
- Avoid lifting head too high during breathing - this causes hips to drop
- Press chest into water to initiate the undulation motion
- Body should ride high in the water with minimal resistance

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Head too high during non-breathing strokes
- Hips sinking due to poor core engagement
- Looking forward instead of down during glide
- Tension in neck and shoulders

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Float and Press** | Lie flat on water surface, press chest down rhythmically while keeping legs relaxed. |
| **Body Dolphin (Arms at Sides)** | Keep arms at sides, perform undulating body motion with no arm movement. |
| **Streamline Kicking** | Hold streamline position (arms extended overhead), practice body undulation without arm movement. |

## Competitive Metrics
#### Drill 1: Butterfly Streamline Glide (15m push-off)
**Self-Check:** Glide distance from push-off before needing to initiate first dolphin kick

> [!tip] Tiered Glide Distance Targets
> | Level | Glide Distance | Notes |
> |-------|---------------|-------|
> | **Beginner** | 5-8m | Learning streamline position; focus on tight body line |
> | **Intermediate** | 8-10m | Consistent streamline at moderate push-off power |
> | **Advanced** | 10-12m | Strong push-off with efficient glide; approaching FINA 15m limit |
> | **Elite** | 12-15m | Maximum utilization of underwater distance within FINA rules |
>
> *Note: Breaststroke achieves the longest glide/pullout distance due to the specialized underwater pullout technique; butterfly glide is shorter but still significant.*

- Self-check cue: Feel your speed dropping as glide decelerates; initiate the first dolphin kick when you can no longer maintain a streamlined glide (approximately 1.2-1.5 m/s depending on push-off speed)

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Hip height: ≤4cm below water surface during full glide (Beginner: ≤8cm, Intermediate: ≤6cm)
- Streamline alignment: Head fully between biceps, arms pressed straight together, core tight
- Body angle: ≤8 degrees from horizontal during glide (flatter than freestyle)

**Competitive Impact:** 1m longer glide per push-off = ~0.35s saved per 100m butterfly race.

#### Drill 2: Prone Dolphin Kick Drill (4x25m, kick only, arms at sides, no pull)
**Self-Check:** Time per 25m, kick count per lap

> [!tip] Tiered Prone Dolphin Kick Targets
> | Level | Time per 25m | Kick Count | Notes |
> |-------|-------------|------------|-------|
> | **Beginner** | 40-50s | 25-35 kicks | Learning hip-driven undulation; focus on form |
> | **Intermediate** | 30-40s | 18-25 kicks | Developing power and efficiency |
> | **Advanced** | 25-30s | 14-18 kicks | Efficient hip-driven kick; good propulsion |
> | **Elite** | 18-22s | 10-14 kicks | Maximal power per kick; optimal amplitude |
>
> *Note: Surface dolphin kick with arms at sides is slower than full butterfly stroke. FINA limit of 15m underwater applies only to starts/turns, not surface drills.*

- Self-check cue: Feel your entire body undulating as a single unit, not just your legs kicking

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Undulation amplitude: ≤30cm from highest to lowest point of hips (Beginner: up to 40cm acceptable)
- No leg-only kick: Upper body undulates in sync with legs
- Hips break water surface on each down kick

**Competitive Impact:** Proper body undulation reduces drag by improving body alignment and streamlining. Research shows submerging just 0.5-1m below the surface reduces total drag by approximately 20-30% by eliminating wave drag (Marinho et al.). The exact drag reduction percentage from undulation alone is difficult to isolate and depends on the swimmer's technique.
