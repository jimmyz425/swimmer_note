## Overview

Breathing in breaststroke must be tightly coordinated with the arm pull and kick cycle. The breath is taken during the insweep phase, letting the head rise naturally with the upper body lift from the pull. A quick inhale is followed by the face returning to the water before the kick completes. The standard pattern is to breathe every stroke for most distances, though sprint events (50-100m) may use every-other-stroke breathing to maintain a lower profile.

**Difficulty:** Moderate

## Key Points to Remember

- **Breathe during the insweep phase** - let head rise naturally with upper body lift from pull
- **Quick inhale, then face back in water** - minimize time head is above surface (typically 0.2-0.5 seconds)
- **Exhale underwater during glide and pull setup** - never hold breath underwater
- **Chin just above water surface** - over-lifting causes hip drop through see-saw effect
- **Timing: inhale begins as hands start insweep** - face returns to water before kick completes
- **Standard pattern: breathe every stroke** - for most distances; sprint (50-100m) may use every-other-stroke

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Breathing after the pull is complete** - timing must be during insweep for natural lift
- **Lifting head too high** - causes immediate hip drop and increases drag by 15-25%
- **Keeping head up too long** - disrupts rhythm and body position
- **Holding breath underwater** - creates tension, wastes energy
- **Over-lifting (chin well above water)** - causes hip drop, breaks streamline

## Specific Drills

| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Timing Count Drill** | Say aloud: "Pull... Breathe... Kick... Glide". 4x25m. |
| **Head-Up Breaststroke** | Keep head above water. Builds neck strength and timing. 2x25m. |
| **Breathe-Count Drill** | Swim counting strokes per breath. Progress to every-other-stroke. |
| **Quick Breath Drill** | Focus on minimizing time face is out of water. 4x25m. |

## Competitive Metrics

#### Drill 1: Rhythm Breathing Drill (4x100m, breathe every stroke)

**Self-Check:** Time per 100m, stroke count per lap

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Breathing Efficiency — Time Difference: Every Stroke vs Every 2nd Stroke)
> - **Beginner:** 5-10% time difference (breathing significantly disrupts rhythm)
> - **Intermediate:** 3-5% time difference
> - **Advanced:** 2-3% time difference
> - **Elite:** ≤2% time difference (breathing barely affects pace)
>
> Self-check cue: Inhale during the insweep phase, exhale fully during kick and glide
>
> **Note:** The ≤2% figure is an elite-level benchmark; competitive swimmers may show larger differences. For sprint events where breathing is minimized, every-other-stroke breathing should show minimal slowdown.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Head lift: **≤10-15cm** above water surface (chin just clearing the surface). Elite sprinters minimize this further. *(Note: exact cm measurement requires calibrated video; coaching cue is "chin just above water.")*
- Breathing duration: Quick inhale, typically **0.2-0.5 seconds**. *(Note: the "≤0.3s" target is achievable by elite sprinters but is not a universal standard; most competitive swimmers have slightly longer breathing windows.)*
- Competitive target: Head returns to neutral position before kick starts

**Competitive Impact:** Lifting the head too high drops the hips through a "see-saw" effect around the center of buoyancy. Research shows poor head position can increase drag by 15-25%, costing significant time per 100m. The exact time cost depends on race distance and swimmer morphology. *(Note: specific cm-to-cm ratios and "40% drag increase" claims found in some coaching sources are approximations; the actual relationship is non-linear and swimmer-dependent.)*

#### Drill 2: No Breathe Drill (4x25m, no breathing, full stroke)

**Self-Check:** Time difference between no-breath 25m vs normal 25m

> [!note] Tiered Targets (No-Breath vs Normal 25m Time Difference)
> - **Beginner:** ≤15% time difference (breathing causes significant rhythm disruption)
> - **Intermediate:** ≤10% time difference
> - **Advanced:** ≤5% time difference
> - **Elite:** ≤3% time difference (breathing has minimal impact on speed)
>
> Self-check cue: Keep your head low, use the pull motion to naturally lift your body for air
>
> **Note:** For sprint events where breathing is minimized, no-breath swims may be faster. For distance events, well-timed breathing should not slow the swimmer significantly.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- No body lift: Shoulders rise minimally above water during breathing (coaching cue: keep head low, use pull to naturally lift body)
- No hip drop: Hips stay near surface during breathing. *(Note: the "7cm" measurement is difficult to verify with phone video; focus on visual alignment.)*
- Competitive target: Stroke length remains identical to breathing strokes

**Competitive Impact:** Efficient breathing technique reduces energy expenditure during races by minimizing disruption to body position and rhythm. The commonly cited "15% reduction" is a coaching estimate; actual savings depend on how much the swimmer's body position is disrupted by breathing. *(Note: no controlled study has isolated breathing technique's energy cost from other variables.)*
