## Overview
Breathing in butterfly occurs during the push phase of the arm stroke, lifting the chin just above the water while keeping the head low. A quick inhale followed by continuous underwater exhale maintains body alignment. Poor breathing timing disrupts the undulation rhythm and causes the hips to drop.
**Difficulty: Moderate to Hard**

## Key Points to Remember
- Breathe during the **PUSH phase** of arm stroke
- Lift chin just above water - do NOT lift head high
- Inhale quickly, exhale continuously underwater
- Breathe every stroke OR every other stroke (based on race distance/fitness)
- Head returns to water BEFORE arms complete recovery
- Keep eyes looking slightly down/forward, not up at ceiling

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Lifting head too high (hips drop)
- Breathing too early (during catch)
- Breathing too late (rushes recovery)
- Holding breath underwater
- Lifting head too slowly
- Looking forward/up instead of down

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Breathing Pattern Practice** | Swim breathing every 2 strokes, then every stroke, then every 3 strokes. |
| **No-Breath Swimming** | Swim butterfly without breathing for short distances (10-15m). Builds lung capacity. |
| **Chin on Surface** | Practice breathing while keeping chin just touching water surface. |
| **Single-Arm Breathing Drill** | Swim single-arm butterfly, practice breathing on the stroking side. |

## Competitive Metrics
#### Drill 1: Rhythm Breathing Drill (4x200m, breathe every 2nd stroke)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between breathing every 1st vs every 2nd stroke

> [!tip] Tiered Breathing Rhythm Disruption
> | Level | Time Difference (1st vs 2nd stroke breathing) | Notes |
> |-------|-----------------------------------------------|-------|
> | **Beginner** | 8-12% | Breathing significantly disrupts rhythm |
> | **Intermediate** | 4-8% | Improving breathing efficiency |
> | **Advanced** | 2-4% | Minimal disruption from breathing |
> | **Elite** | ≤2% | Breathing seamlessly integrated into stroke |

- Self-check cue: Breathe during the push phase of the pull, not during the catch

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Head lift: ≤10cm above water (Beginner: up to 15cm acceptable as technique develops)
- Breathing duration: ~0.3-0.5s per breath (quick, efficient inhale; Beginner: up to 0.8s)
- Head returns to neutral position before hands enter water

**Competitive Impact:** Lifting the head too high during breathing breaks the body undulation rhythm, causes the hips to drop, and increases drag. The "~1s per 100m" cost for 5cm excess head lift is a coaching estimate; the actual cost depends on how the head lift affects overall body position and how many breaths are taken per race.

#### Drill 2: No Breathe Drill (4x25m, no breathing, full stroke)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between no-breath 25m vs normal 25m

> [!tip] Tiered Breathing Impact on Speed
> | Level | Time Difference (no-breath vs normal) | Notes |
> |-------|--------------------------------------|-------|
> | **Beginner** | 10-15% | Breathing significantly reduces power |
> | **Intermediate** | 6-10% | Improving breathing efficiency |
> | **Advanced** | 3-6% | Good breathing technique |
> | **Elite** | ≤3-6% | Breathing has minimal speed cost |

- Self-check cue: Keep your chin just above water, use the undulation to naturally lift your body for air

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- No body lift: Shoulders rise ≤12cm above water during breathing
- No hip drop: Hips stay within 6cm of surface during breathing (Beginner: within 10cm)
- Undulation rhythm remains identical between breathing and non-breathing strokes

**Competitive Impact:** Efficient breathing technique -- keeping the head low and breathing during the push phase -- reduces the energy cost of each breathing cycle. The "20% reduction" figure is an overestimate; efficient breathing reduces the disruption to body position and rhythm, which conserves energy over the race distance. The actual energy savings depend on how much head lift the swimmer typically uses.
