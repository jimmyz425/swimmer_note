## Overview
Body position is the foundation for all other backstroke techniques. A flat, horizontal body with hips riding high reduces drag and improves efficiency. This is the easiest technique to master and serves as the base upon which everything else is built.

**Difficulty:** Easiest (Foundation for all other techniques)

## Key Points to Remember
- **Keep body flat and horizontal** - Reduces drag, improves efficiency
- **Head neutral, looking up** - Head position controls hip position; water line at ears
- **Press chest slightly up** - Keeps hips near surface
- **Hips should ride high** - Prevents "sitting" in water
- **Core engaged throughout** - Maintains alignment

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Head too high** - Lifts upper body, drops hips (creates drag). Fix: Keep head neutral, ears in water
- **Head too deep** - Causes water to wash over face
- **Sitting position** - Hips dropped, legs drag. Fix: Engage core, press chest up
- **Arching back** - Strains lower back, poor alignment
- **Looking around nervously** - Disrupts head position
- **Looking toward feet** - Creates drag, poor alignment. Fix: Look straight up
- **Head moving side-to-side** - Unstable body position. Fix: Keep head still throughout stroke

## Specific Drills

| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Torpedo Float** | Float on back, arms streamlined, focus on hip position |
| **6-Beat Kick on Back** | Kick on back with arms at sides, feel body rise |
| **Balance Drill** | Float on back, press chest up to raise hips |
| **Head Position Check** | Swim with small cup on forehead - keep it balanced |
| **Streamline Back Kick** | Tight streamline position, kick to maintain body line |

## Competitive Drills

#### Drill 1: Streamline Back Push-offs (15m wall)
**Self-Check:** Count glide duration from push-off until you need to initiate first kick/stroke

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Glide Distance/Time Before Speed Decay)
> - **Beginner:** 3-5m or ~3-4 seconds before speed drops below useful pace
> - **Intermediate:** 5-7m or ~4-5 seconds
> - **Advanced:** 7-10m or ~5-6 seconds
> - **Elite:** 10-12m+ or ~6-8 seconds
>
> Why measure distance, not time? A push-off starts at ~2.0-2.5 m/s but decelerates rapidly due to quadratic drag. By 5m, speed typically drops to ~1.5-1.7 m/s even with a tight streamline. Time-to-distance depends on your initial push-off force, making it an unreliable metric for glide *quality*.
>
> **Note on drag comparison:** Backstroke and freestyle streamlines have **similar** drag coefficients — there is no significant "lower drag profile" in backstroke. Both strokes in tight streamline achieve comparable glide distances. Push-off speed values are consistent with biomechanics literature (Lyttle et al., 1998; 1999).

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Hip height: Hips at or near shoulder line during full glide (relative measure, more practical than absolute depth). **Note:** The ≤3cm absolute target requires calibrated camera setup; use visual assessment instead.
- Streamline alignment: Head fully between biceps, arms pressed straight together, no back arch
- Competitive target: 100% of push-offs maintain tight streamline through the full glide phase

**Competitive Impact:** Better push-off technique saves ~0.05-0.1s per wall. In short course (25m pool): 4 push-offs per 100m (start + 3 turns), saving ~0.2-0.4s. In long course (50m pool): 2 push-offs per 100m (start + 1 turn), saving ~0.1-0.2s. *These are coaching estimates based on turn performance studies (Mason et al., 2013). Actual savings vary by swimmer and wall type.*

#### Drill 2: Torpedo Float Hold (still water)
**Self-Check:** Hold perfect flat streamline position without kicking

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≥20 seconds continuous hold
> - **Intermediate:** ≥30 seconds
> - **Advanced:** ≥45 seconds
> - **Elite:** ≥60 seconds, no hip sinking
>
> Self-check cue: If your feet drop below surface, your core is not engaged enough
>
> **Note:** These hold times are coaching benchmarks, not research-validated standards. They are useful as general indicators of body position awareness and core endurance.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Body tilt angle: ≤5 degrees from horizontal (visual estimate — body should appear flat). **Note:** Precise angle measurement requires calibrated camera; use visual assessment for self-coaching.
- No back arch: Spine stays in neutral position, no lumbar curvature
- Competitive target: Ears remain at water surface for entire hold

**Competitive Impact:** Flat body position significantly reduces drag compared to "sitting" backstroke position. *The 25% drag reduction figure cited in some coaching literature represents total frontal drag increase from severe body position errors (dropped hips + head lift combined), not from head lift alone. CFD studies (Bixler & Rieweg, 1998; Bixler et al., 2007) suggest body position errors can increase drag by 15-25%, but exact values vary by body type and baseline technique.*
