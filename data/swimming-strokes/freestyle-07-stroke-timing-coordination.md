## Overview
Stroke timing and coordination is the master skill that brings all individual techniques together. It involves maintaining a continuous stroke cycle with opposing arm positions, synchronized kick rhythm, and seamless breathing integration. Modern freestyle has minimal glide — the stroke should be continuous and rhythmic.
**Difficulty: Hard**

## Key Points to Remember
- **Opposing arm positions** - When one arm enters, other is pulling
- **Continuous stroke cycle** - No pauses, always moving
- **Kick rhythm matches stroke** - 2-beat, 4-beat, or 6-beat timing with arms
- **Rotation timing** - Rotation peaks when arm reaches full extension
- **Breathing integration** - Breathe during natural rotation window
- **No "glide" phase** - Modern freestyle has minimal glide; stroke is continuous

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Catch-up pause** - Arms waiting for each other instead of continuous opposition
- **Gliding too long** - Extended pause after entry (old-school mistake)
- **Kick timing off** - Kicks not synchronized with rotation/stroke
- **Hitching** - Pause or hesitation in stroke cycle
- **Breathing breaks rhythm** - Stroke changes significantly when breathing
- **Arms not opposing** - Both arms doing similar things instead of opposite phases

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **6-3-6 Timing** | 6 kicks side, 3 strokes, 6 kicks other side. Develops rhythm and timing awareness. |
| **Continuous Stroke** | Focus on eliminating any pauses in stroke cycle. |
| **2-Beat Kick Timing** | Practice one kick per stroke (kick when arm enters). |
| **6-Beat Kick Timing** | Practice six kicks per stroke cycle (classic rhythm). |
| **Rhythm Swimming** | Swim with metronome or music to develop consistent tempo. |

## Competitive Drills
#### Drill 1: Metronome Swimming (4x200m set, use waterproof metronome or audible pool-side metronome)
**Self-Check:** Perceived tempo consistency and stroke count stability

> [!note] Targets
> - **Beginner/Intermediate:** Stay within ±1 beep of the metronome for most of the set
> - **Advanced:** Consistently sync entry with metronome beep for ≥90% of strokes
> - **Elite:** ≥95% of strokes synced within one metronome interval
>
> Self-check cue: Sync your hand entry exactly with the metronome beep. If you consistently hear the beep mid-stroke or mid-recovery, your tempo is off.
>
> **Note:** These sync percentages are coaching guidelines. Metronome training is widely used (e.g., by Total Immersion and SwimSmooth programs) to develop consistent stroke rhythm. The ≥90%/≥95% targets reflect a progression from occasional drift to consistent rhythm maintenance.

> [!tip] Metronome Precision Note
> Claims of "±0.05s precision" require electronic measurement. For self-coaching, the metronome interval itself (typically 0.5-1.0s per beat at typical stroke rates) is your precision unit. Focus on consistent rhythm, not millisecond accuracy.

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Kick-to-stroke ratio: Visible synchronization between kick cycle and arm cycle (6-beat: 6 kicks per 2 strokes; 2-beat: 2 kicks per 2 strokes; 4-beat: 4 kicks per 2 strokes). **Note:** Kick patterns should match event demands — 6-beat for sprints, 2-beat or 4-beat for distance. Some elite swimmers use hybrid patterns that shift between 2-beat and 6-beat within a race.
- No glide: ≤0.1s pause between entry and catch (visible as continuous motion — no "stop and go" pattern). **Note:** Measuring 0.1s pauses requires ≥30fps video minimum; ≥120fps is preferable for reliable measurement. For practical self-coaching, assess qualitatively: does the stroke flow continuously, or is there a noticeable "dead spot" where the hand waits before catching?
- Competitive target: No rhythm disruption during breathing (stroke timing consistent whether breathing or not)

**Competitive Impact:** Tempo variation costs time per 100m. Even small inconsistencies accumulate over a race distance. *The exact time cost depends on stroke rate and race distance, but maintaining consistent tempo is a hallmark of efficient swimmers.*

#### Drill 2: Ankle Band Swimming (2x100m, no kick allowed)
**Self-Check:** Time difference between swimming with vs without ankle band

> [!note] Tiered Targets
> - **Beginner:** ≤20% time increase when kick is eliminated
> - **Intermediate:** ≤15% time increase
> - **Advanced/Elite:** ≤10% time increase (shows efficient pull mechanics)
>
> Self-check cue: Focus on pulling hard with your core and lats, not just arms. The band will eliminate kick — feel the pull doing all the work.
>
> **Note:** These percentages represent *time increase*, not propulsion loss. Because the pull buoy provides additional buoyancy (reducing drag from the legs), the time increase from eliminating kick is typically less than the kick's propulsion contribution percentage. Also, the pull can compensate somewhat by generating more force per stroke. These values are coaching benchmarks and vary significantly by individual.

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Opposing arm motion: When one arm enters, the other is mid-pull (no catch-up pause — arms in opposite phases)
- No hip movement: Hips stay stable (no kicking motion — band should eliminate this)
- Competitive target: Stroke count increase ≤5% vs normal swimming

**Competitive Impact:** Pull efficiency is the single largest determinant of sprint swimming speed. Ankle band swimming isolates pull technique and reveals how much you rely on kick vs. pull for propulsion. *Pull contributes approximately 70-80% of race propulsion in sprint events (kick 20-30%) and 85-90% in distance events (kick 10-15%). These figures are based on the active drag measurement method (Zamparo et al., 1996) and have been supported by subsequent studies, though individual variation is significant. The ratio varies by gender, event, and individual kick strength. Some elite sprinters (e.g., Caeleb Dressel) generate substantially more than 30% of their propulsion from their kick.*
