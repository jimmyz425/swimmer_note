## Overview
The arm recovery sweeps arms low over the water surface with relaxed, slightly bent arms leading with thumbs. A quick but controlled recovery sets the stroke tempo while minimizing drag and energy waste. Tension or high recovery during this phase causes fatigue and slows the stroke.
**Difficulty: Moderate**

## Key Points to Remember
- Sweep arms low over the water surface
- Keep arms relaxed and slightly bent during recovery
- Lead with thumbs, hands rotate outward
- Recovery should be quick but controlled
- Avoid high, sweeping recovery (too slow, causes fatigue)
- Hands should enter water before arms are fully extended

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Arms too high during recovery (slow, tiring)
- Straight, rigid arms during recovery
- Throwing water backwards during recovery
- Pausing at end of recovery
- Over-rotating shoulders
- Tension in arms during recovery

## Specific Drills
| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Low Recovery Drill** | Exaggerate low, wide recovery with bent arms. Focus on keeping hands close to water surface. |
| **Dive Entry Drill** | Practice entering hands first in a diving motion, then sweeping forward underwater. |
| **Relaxed Recovery Swim** | Swim butterfly focusing entirely on arm relaxation during recovery. Let momentum carry arms. |

## Competitive Drills
#### Drill 1: Thumb Drag Drill (4x50m set, drag thumbs along water surface during recovery)
**Self-Check:** Count percentage of strokes where your thumbs drag along water surface during recovery

> [!tip] Tiered Thumb Drag Consistency
> | Level | Thumb Drag % | Notes |
> |-------|-------------|-------|
> | **Beginner** | 50-70% | Learning low recovery; arms tend to lift too high |
> | **Intermediate** | 70-85% | Improving arm path; consistent on shorter distances |
> | **Advanced** | 85-95% | Near-consistent low recovery |
> | **Elite** | 95-100% | Low, relaxed recovery at all speeds |

- Self-check cue: Feel the water on your thumbs the entire recovery path, keep arms relaxed

**Video Check (front-angle footage):**
- Recovery height: Hands travel ≤10cm above water surface (Beginner: up to 20cm acceptable)
- Recovery width: Hands stay within shoulder width during recovery (no wide swing)
- Arms relaxed, elbows slightly bent during recovery

**Competitive Impact:** High/wide recovery creates significantly more frontal drag and wastes energy. Keeping arms low and close to the water surface during recovery reduces air resistance and the energy cost of lifting arms against gravity. The "30% more drag" figure is an estimate; the actual increase depends on recovery height and speed.

#### Drill 2: Quick Entry Drill (4x100m set, focus on fast hand entry)
**Self-Check:** Stroke tempo (strokes per minute)

> [!tip] Tiered Stroke Rate Targets (cycles per minute)
> | Level | Sprint 50m | 100m | 200m |
> |-------|-----------|------|------|
> | **Beginner** | 25-30 | 22-28 | 20-25 |
> | **Intermediate** | 30-38 | 28-34 | 25-30 |
> | **Advanced** | 38-45 | 33-40 | 30-36 |
> | **Elite** | 45-48 | 40-45 | 35-38 |
>
> *Note: Stroke rate typically decreases over race distance. Final 25m often sees a 5-10% drop from peak rate due to fatigue. Elite 50m sprinters may reach 45-48 cycles/min; 200m emphasizes lower stroke rates with greater distance per stroke.*

- Self-check cue: Enter hands into water immediately after exit, no hesitation during recovery

**Video Check (side-angle footage):**
- Recovery speed: Recovery phase is notably faster than the pull phase (quick, snappy recovery). The "2x faster" ratio is a coaching heuristic; actual ratio varies with stroke rate.
- Entry timing: Hands enter water as the first down kick of the cycle begins (synchronized with entry)
- Minimal splash on entry (indicates clean hand placement)

**Competitive Impact:** Faster, more efficient recovery allows higher stroke tempo, contributing to faster race speed. The claim that faster recovery increases stroke tempo by 10% leading to ~5% faster race speed is a coaching estimate; the actual relationship depends on maintaining technique quality at higher tempos.
