## Overview

The breaststroke arm pull consists of four distinct phases: Catch, Outsweep, Insweep, and Recovery/Shoot. The pull should be kept narrow and efficient — pulling too wide wastes energy, while pulling too deep sinks the hips. Elbows must stay higher than hands during the insweep (power phase), and the recovery shoots arms forward quickly back to streamlined position. The entire motion should be smooth and continuous with no pausing between phases.

**Difficulty:** Moderate

## Key Points to Remember

- **Catch (Setup): arms at full extension** - palms turn outward to approximately 45 degrees to establish grip on the water
- **Outsweep: narrow movement** - hands move outward just past shoulder width; elite swimmers may pull slightly wider depending on body proportions
- **Insweep (Power Phase): elbows stay higher than hands** - hands sweep inward toward chest, accelerate during this phase, do not pull deeper than chest level
- **Recovery/Shoot: arms drive forward quickly** - return to streamlined position, hands should not go past hip line
- **Keep pull narrow and efficient** - pulling too wide wastes energy
- **Hands above chest level** - pulling too deep sinks hips
- **Accelerate hands throughout** - smooth, continuous motion with no pausing between phases

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Pulling too wide** - wastes energy, extends outside body's streamline
- **Pulling too deep** - hands below chest level sinks hips
- **Dropping elbows during insweep** - elbows must stay higher than hands for efficient catch
- **Pulling too slowly** - reduces propulsion, breaks rhythm
- **Pausing between phases** - should be smooth, continuous motion

## Specific Drills

| Drill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Standing Arm Pull** | Stand in shallow water, practice pull motion. 20-30 repetitions slow and controlled. |
| **Pull with Buoy** | Hold pull buoy between legs, swim using only arms. 4x25m with rest. |
| **Catch-Up Pull Drill** | Complete one full pull cycle, pause in glide. Repeat. 4x25m. |
| **Single-Arm Breaststroke** | Keep one arm extended, pull with other arm. Switch halfway. |
| **Sculling Drill** | Arms at chest level, small sculling motions. 2-3 minutes. |

## Competitive Drills

#### Drill 1: Pull Only Drill (4x50m, pull buoy only, no kick)

**Self-Check:** Distance per stroke (stroke length) and time per 50m

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Pull-Only 50m — Pull Buoy, No Kick)
> - **Beginner:** ≥1.0m per stroke, ≤60 seconds per 50m (LCM)
> - **Intermediate:** ≥1.2m per stroke, ≤55 seconds per 50m
> - **Advanced:** ≥1.5m per stroke, ≤45 seconds per 50m
> - **Elite:** ≥1.8m per stroke, ≤40 seconds per 50m
>
> Self-check cue: Feel your hands "catch" water immediately at the start of the pull, no slipping
>
> **Note:** Distance per stroke with pull buoy will be less than full stroke due to lack of kick propulsion. These are coaching benchmarks; actual values depend on arm length and pull efficiency.

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Pull width: Hands sweep out to approximately shoulder width during outsweep; some elite swimmers pull slightly wider. *(Note: "no wider than shoulder width" is a simplification; optimal pull width varies with body proportions.)*
- Insweep angle: Hands come together under chest; elbow bend during insweep is typically **100-140 degrees** depending on pull phase (elbows more extended at catch, more bent at peak insweep). *(Corrected: document previously stated "90 degree elbow bend" which is too precise; actual elbow angle varies throughout the pull cycle.)*
- Competitive target: No over-sweeping (hands never go past shoulder width)

**Competitive Impact:** Proper pull technique generates approximately **30-40% of breaststroke propulsion** in most biomechanical analyses. *(Note: older studies sometimes attributed only 20-30% to the arms; more recent 3D analyses of elite swimmers show the insweep phase can contribute up to 40%, especially during the sprint pullout.)*

#### Drill 2: Sculling Drill (2x100m, breaststroke scull only, no full pull, no kick)

**Self-Check:** Distance traveled per minute of sculling

> [!note] Tiered Targets (Sculling Efficiency)
> - **Beginner:** Steady forward movement, focus on maintaining water feel
> - **Intermediate:** Consistent forward movement, elbows stay above hands
> - **Advanced:** ≥15m distance covered in 1 minute of continuous sculling
> - **Elite:** ≥20m distance in 1 minute, with strong, consistent lift force
>
> Self-check cue: Keep elbows high, feel pressure on your palms and forearms during sculling
>
> **Note:** Sculling drills should be evaluated qualitatively on water feel and consistency rather than purely on distance covered. The distance targets are approximate coaching guidelines.

**Video Check (underwater footage):**
- Scull angle: Hands change angle throughout the sculling motion to maintain lift. The "45-60 degrees" figure is a coaching approximation; actual hand angles vary continuously and are difficult to measure precisely without motion capture.
- No elbow drop: Elbows stay above wrists throughout sculling phase
- Competitive target: Consistent forward movement, no bouncing

**Competitive Impact:** Improving pull efficiency has a meaningful but non-linear effect on overall breaststroke speed. The formula "every 10% pull efficiency increase = 3% faster speed" found in some coaching sources is a rough approximation; actual gains depend on the swimmer's kick strength, body position, and overall technique balance.
